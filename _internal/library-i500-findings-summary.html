The I500 — In-Depth Findings Summary v1.

Aggregate findings from the I500 community audit programme. 53 products catalogued at a single South London independent retail field site, March–April 2026. Every barcode scanned against Open Food Facts. Every label photographed and every nutrition value transcribed directly from the physical pack.

This is the public-tier summary — aggregate findings, structural patterns, headline figures. The row-level corpus (named shops, named brands, named SKUs, audit-date stamping) is part of the I500 dataset, available under institutional licence via the Partner programme.

Cohort note: this v1 findings report is a deep pull on 53 products at a single South London independent retail field site (research period March–April 2026); 58% absent from OFF in this single-site cohort. The broader 155-SKU audit aggregate across multiple South London independent shops sits at 93.2% absent from OFF (strict methodology: 138 of 148 audited SKUs) as of 13 May 2026 (refreshed from the earlier 149-SKU / 84% lenient reading of 4 May 2026 when the methodology shifted to strict per the §50 Honesty Test) — named in the Knowledge Library Stream 1.5 entry. Different cohorts; both figures are correct for what they cover.

The headline finding

58% of products in this community retail environment are completely absent from Open Food Facts.

A further 23% are unregistered, unverifiable, or carry inaccurate data. Only 17% return a result a clinician or patient could rely on.

This matters because Open Food Facts is the database underpinning the NHS Food Scanner and multiple commercial food-tracking apps. The free tier of digital food intervention — what the GP recommends, what the diabetes nurse refers to, what the population-health initiative builds on — is structurally blind to the products real households in this neighbourhood actually buy.

Salt findings

Salt loadings cluster sharply between two structural patterns.

Of 39 products fully classified for salt against FSA per-100g thresholds: 11 (28%) are RED (over 1.5g/100g); 6 (15%) are AMBER (0.3–1.5g); 22 (56%) are GREEN (under 0.3g). The distribution is bimodal — mostly very low salt (whole grains, dried beans, rice) or very high salt (seasoning blends, bouillon cubes, brined sauces). Few products sit in between.

Headline aggregate figures (publishable)

  • Highest salt reading in the cohort: 75g per 100g — effectively three-quarters salt by weight.
  • Bouillon cubes: 55–58.5g salt per 100g across the dominant brand category. A single cube delivers approximately 38% of the NHS daily adult salt limit. Multi-cube use in family cooking is standard.
  • Spice-mix sachets: 23–28.8g salt per 100g across the dominant masala-mix brand category. Used by the full sachet per dish (~50g), this delivers 11.5–14.4g of salt per dish — 192–240% of the NHS daily adult limit, in a single pan.
  • The same-shelf salt trap. Two seasoning products from the same manufacturer, on the same shelf, differ by 50g salt per 100g. The high-salt version carries no front-of-pack warning. Both are absent from Open Food Facts. The distinction exists only on the physical back-of-pack label.

Public-tier discipline: the named brands behind these patterns and the named SKUs are part of the I500 dataset and are available under institutional licence via the Partner programme. The aggregate patterns are the publishable finding; the row-level corpus is the asset.

Sugar findings

The health-halo sugar trap is the structural pattern.

Of 39 products fully classified for sugar against FSA per-100g thresholds: 8 (21%) are RED (over 22.5g/100g); 9 are AMBER (5–22.5g); the remainder GREEN (under 5g).

Headline aggregate figures (publishable)

  • Highest sugar reading in the cohort: 91g per 100g — from a product positioned and marketed as a natural, lower-glycaemic-index alternative to refined sugar.
  • The health-halo cluster. Four of the eight RED-for-sugar products in the cohort are sold with natural / organic / health-alternative positioning — coconut sugar (91g/100g), agave syrups (71–73g/100g), and blackstrap molasses (60g/100g). A patient actively reducing sugar intake may switch to these believing they are healthier choices. The back-of-pack data tells a different story.
  • The porridge breakfast trap. Two flavoured porridge mixes in the cohort contain 36–39g sugar per 100g — 9–10 teaspoons of sugar in a standard 40g bowl. These products are positioned as traditional breakfast staples and are regularly fed to children and elderly family members. Both are absent from Open Food Facts.
Saturated fat & calorie density

Cooking-ingredient densities exceed clinical daily limits in single additions.

Highest saturated fat in the cohort: 65g per 100g (a culinary block-form ingredient typical of regional cooking). Used in volumes of 50–200g per dish — a 50g portion delivers 32.5g saturated fat, more than 1.6 times the NHS daily recommended limit for women (20g) and above the limit for men (30g) in a single cooking addition. Absent from Open Food Facts.

Highest calorie density in the cohort: 884 kcal per 100g (a culinary oil widespread in West African cooking; ~100g fat per 100g). A 15ml tablespoon delivers approximately 7.5g of saturated fat. No barcode registered — no possible database entry.

The US-format labelling gap

7 products in the cohort use US Nutrition Facts panels — sodium in mg per serving, not g of salt per 100g.

UK FSA standards require salt declaration as grams per 100g. US Nutrition Facts panels declare sodium in milligrams per serving. No consumer food-tracking app currently in the UK market converts this at the moment of decision. All 7 products in the cohort using US-format panels are absent from Open Food Facts. All 7 carry high salt readings.

The conversion formula applied in the I500 to bring US-format labels into UK-comparable values: salt (g/100g) = (sodium mg ÷ serving size g) × 2.54 ÷ 10.

Methodology

Field methodology summary.

For each catalogued product: barcode scanned and recorded (EAN where present); product name, brand, and category recorded; nutrition data transcribed per 100g from the physical label (sugar, salt, saturated fat, calories); FSA traffic-light rating calculated; sugar converted to teaspoons per serving (4g per teaspoon); salt converted to sachets per serving (0.5g per sachet); Open Food Facts status checked; label photographed for audit trail.

Verification standards

  • Verified — back label photographed. Nutrition values transcribed directly from the UK-format label. Highest confidence tier. 38 active records.
  • Verified — US panel, salt extrapolated. US-format panels express sodium in mg per serving. Conversion formula applied (above). All seasoning-blend US-panel products in the cohort.
  • Partial. One product requires return visit to confirm brand identity. Nutrition values verified; brand obscured on label.

The structural conclusion

No digital tool can close this gap without field research.

The products are on the shelf. The labels are legible. The barcodes exist. The data does not. Open Food Facts depends on community contribution; community contribution skews toward the products mainstream contributors shop for; the products in the field site catalogued here are not mainstream-contributor shopping. The structural gap is a function of how the open database was built, not a flaw in any specific tool.

The I500 community audit programme is the structured intervention that closes this gap product by product, with shop-owner consent, by trained community auditors. The methodology is repeatable. The dataset compounds. The published findings (this report) describe the structural condition; the row-level corpus is what licensed institutional buyers access for their own analytical work.
