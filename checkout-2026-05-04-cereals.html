Class 3 · The Weekly Supermarket Checkout, Decoded · Inaugural · Monday 4 May 2026

Ten cereals on shelves this week. Read what’s really in them.

The boxes on the breakfast aisle this morning declared everything. The data was on the back of the pack. None of it was visible at the shelf in the units a person making a decision actually uses — teaspoons of sugar, half-gram salt sachets, FSA traffic lights, per-portion versus per-100g divergence. We translated.

Data captured Monday 4 May 2026, 09:00 BST. Methodology: scansmart.uk/methodology.

The structural finding

Per-100g is worse than the per-portion picture suggests.

This week’s ten best-selling cereals across UK supermarkets carry between three and twelve teaspoons of sugar per portion. The per-100g picture is worse on most of them than the per-portion picture suggests — small portions hide large concentrations. Two of the ten clear the FSA red threshold on sugar at both per-100g and per-portion. Three more clear it at per-100g but read amber per-portion because the declared serving size is small.

We are not telling you what to buy. We are telling you what is on the back of the pack, in the units the moment of decision uses.

Pattern reading

Three findings worth naming together.

1. The per-100g picture is worse than the per-portion picture suggests.

Cereal portion sizes are small (usually 30 to 45 grams) and the per-portion sugar declaration looks acceptable on packs that are concentrated when measured per-100g. The FSA per-100g threshold for red on sugar is 22.5 grams; several cereals in the top ten clear it. The per-portion threshold is 27 grams; fewer clear it because the serving size is small. Both numbers are on the back of the pack. Both are honest. The structural point is which one is visible at the moment of decision and which one is not.

2. Reformulation has moved on some products and not on others.

Two of the cereals in the top ten carry a lower per-100g sugar declaration than they did in 2024 (we have the prior dated audits on file). One has reformulated upward. Six are unchanged. The DHSC voluntary sugar reduction programme has produced movement on some products in the category and not on others, and the public per-100g figures are how to track which is which.

3. The “healthier” framing reads differently when translated.

Granolas — sometimes positioned as a healthier choice — carry sugar concentrations comparable to or higher than some classically-positioned high-sugar cereals. Bran-based cereals carry surprising salt loads. The front-of-pack visual cues do not always map to the back-of-pack data, and the structural distance is the point.

The structural critique here names the manufacturer’s role honestly. The shopkeeper is not the villain. The retailer is not the villain. The manufacturer is the creator of the gap — they source the ingredients (rolled oats, cane sugar, palm oil, the synthetic vitamins enabling the now fortified claim), they employ the food scientists who engineer the per-portion vs per-100g divergence, they decide the serving size declared on the side of the box, they fund the research that biases the nutrition science around their own products. The structural gap between back-of-pack and moment-of-decision did not appear by accident. The structural critique protects the shopkeeper standing at the till and names the manufacturer who engineered the gap. Full citation register: Industry Funding Bias evidence vault.

What this piece does and does not do

The decision is yours.

This piece reads what is on the back of the pack and translates it into teaspoons, sachets, and FSA traffic lights. It does not tell you whether to buy any particular cereal. It does not give clinical or medical advice. It does not campaign against any manufacturer or retailer. It documents, and it translates, and it shows you the structural gap between the data on the pack and the units the moment of decision uses. The decision itself is yours.

If you have type 2 diabetes, hypertension, or any other condition that makes specific dietary thresholds matter to you, your GP or a registered dietitian is the right conversation for advice. SCANSMART does not replace that conversation; it gives you the back-of-pack data in units you can read fast.
