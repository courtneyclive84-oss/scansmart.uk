Supplements — Evidence Vault.

Source-validated peer-reviewed evidence on food supplements. Supplements occupy a regulatory gap between medicines (MHRA) and food (FSA), and the consumer is asked to interpret claims, doses, and interactions with less institutional scaffolding than either tier supplies. Two load-bearing realities anchor the brief: broad-spectrum supplementation in non-deficient adults shows null or trivial effects on hard endpoints in the better-designed trials (USPSTF 2022 JAMA; Cochrane antioxidants); deficiency-targeted supplementation — folic acid pre-conception, vitamin D in deficiency, B12 in vegans and older adults, iron in iron-deficiency anaemia — has clear evidence and clinical-guideline backing. The structural critique sits in the gap: the claim environment outruns the trial evidence, and the consumer carries the interpretive load.

⭐ The headline finding

USPSTF 2022: insufficient evidence for most supplements in primary prevention; recommend AGAINST beta-carotene and vitamin E for CVD and cancer prevention.

Primary source. US Preventive Services Task Force. Vitamin, Mineral, and Multivitamin Supplementation to Prevent Cardiovascular Disease and Cancer: US Preventive Services Task Force Recommendation Statement. JAMA 2022;327(23):2326–2333.

Tagged: trial evidence. Randomised-controlled-trial endpoints in community-dwelling non-pregnant adults, not observational nutritional epidemiology. Pregnancy supplementation, paediatric supplementation, and supplementation for clinical deficiency are explicitly out of scope of the USPSTF statement and sit on separate evidence bases.

What the recommendation says.

  • 'I' statement (insufficient evidence) for most vitamins, minerals, and multivitamins for primary prevention of CVD and cancer in community-dwelling, non-pregnant adults.
  • 'D' statement (recommend AGAINST) for beta-carotene for primary prevention of CVD and cancer — based on signal of increased lung-cancer risk in smokers (ATBC, CARET trials).
  • 'D' statement (recommend AGAINST) for vitamin E for primary prevention of CVD and cancer.
  • Folic acid for pre-conception NTD prevention sits on a separate, positive recommendation (USPSTF 2017, current).

Why this matters. [Prose to draft for v1.0: the recommendation translates a four-decade RCT evidence base into a population-level prevention frame. The category-collapse the brief is dismantling is ‘supplements work / don’t work’ — the actual evidence-base distinction is between deficiency-correction (works, clinical-pathway-managed) and broad-spectrum optimisation in non-deficient adults (doesn’t, and beta-carotene / high-dose vitamin E in smokers shows signal of harm). The Bjelakovic Cochrane antioxidant meta-analysis reaches the same conclusion on all-cause mortality. The structural read: the regulatory architecture lets the claim environment outrun the trial evidence.]

Citation. USPSTF. JAMA 2022;327(23):2326–2333. Bjelakovic G et al. Antioxidant supplements for prevention of mortality. Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2012;CD007176.

COSMOS and VITAL — the largest recent RCTs

COSMOS 2022 and VITAL 2019: no meaningful effect on CVD or invasive cancer at population scale.

Primary sources. Sesso HD, et al. (2022). Multivitamins in the Prevention of Cancer and Cardiovascular Disease (COSMOS). Am J Clin Nutr 2022;115(6):1501–1510. Manson JE, et al. (2019). Vitamin D Supplements and Prevention of Cancer and Cardiovascular Disease (VITAL). N Engl J Med 2019;380(1):33–44.

Tagged: trial evidence. Two large pragmatic RCTs in US adult populations, both reading on primary-prevention hard endpoints.

What the trials say.

  • COSMOS (n ≈ 21,000): daily multivitamin (Centrum Silver) vs placebo. No significant effect on major cardiovascular events; no significant effect on total invasive cancer.
  • VITAL (n ≈ 26,000): vitamin D₃ 2000 IU/day and/or marine omega-3 1g/day vs placebo. No significant effect on primary endpoints of invasive cancer or major cardiovascular events.
  • Secondary signals — VITAL: a possible small reduction in cancer mortality emerging over longer follow-up (not the primary endpoint); inconsistent across analyses.

Why this matters. [Prose to draft for v1.0: COSMOS and VITAL are the trials cited verbatim in the USPSTF 2022 evidence review. Together they close most of the population-level primary-prevention case for multivitamin and vitamin-D supplementation in non-deficient adults. Note the inferential limit: both trials enrolled US adults; baseline vitamin D status in VITAL was ‘sufficient’ for most participants by US 25(OH)D thresholds. The deficiency-correction case in baseline-deficient populations is not what these trials tested.]

Citation. Sesso HD et al. Am J Clin Nutr 2022;115(6):1501–1510. Manson JE et al. N Engl J Med 2019;380(1):33–44.

Where the evidence is positive

Folic acid pre-conception, vitamin D in deficiency, B12 in vegans and older adults, iron in IDA: the deficiency-targeted gold-standard cases.

Primary sources. MRC Vitamin Study Research Group. Prevention of neural tube defects: results of the Medical Research Council Vitamin Study. Lancet 1991;338(8760):131–137. SACN 2016 Vitamin D and Health. NICE PH56 Vitamin D. British Society for Haematology 2014 cobalamin and folate guidelines. NICE iron-deficiency anaemia pathway; SACN 2010 Iron and Health.

Tagged: deficiency-targeted evidence. Defined population × defined endpoint × time-limited or condition-managed supplementation. Distinct from broad-spectrum prevention supplementation in non-deficient adults.

Folic acid pre-conception. [Prose to draft for v1.0: MRC 1991 RCT showed ~72% reduction in NTD recurrence with 4 mg/day folic acid. The wider 400 µg/day pre-conception standard derives from this and subsequent observational and quasi-experimental evidence. UK current recommendation: 400 µg/day pre-conception through 12 weeks gestation. UK mandatory flour fortification decision 2021–2024 implementation. The cleanest deficiency-targeted supplementation case in the published evidence base.]

Vitamin D in deficiency. [Prose to draft for v1.0: NICE PH56 universal 10 µg/day October–March recommendation. SACN 2016. Ethnicity-stratified deficiency prevalence (Cashman 2016 AJCN). The ‘replete vs deficient’ framing (Heaney 2014): deficiency-correction has evidence; supraphysiological dosing in non-deficient adults does not, and VITAL 2019 closed most of the latter case for primary CVD / cancer prevention.]

B12 in vegans and older adults. [Prose to draft for v1.0: BSH 2014 guideline. Serum B12 / methylmalonic acid diagnostic pathway. Pernicious anaemia and intrinsic-factor case. Oral high-dose vs intramuscular cyanocobalamin. The NICE-aligned diagnosis-then-supplement sequence.]

Iron in iron-deficiency anaemia. [Prose to draft for v1.0: NICE pathway; haemoglobin / ferritin diagnostic threshold; ferrous fumarate vs ferrous bisglycinate vs ferrous sulphate; the alternate-day dosing evidence (Stoffel 2017 Lancet Haematology); the GI side-effect tolerability problem; the contrasting case — universal iron supplementation in non-deficient adults shows null or adverse signals.]

Why this matters. [Prose to draft for v1.0: each of these is a specific population × specific endpoint × clinical-pathway-supervised case. The fact that broad-spectrum primary-prevention supplementation is null does not transfer to these cases, and vice versa. The structural-pattern failure mode is mixing the two — selling deficiency-case evidence as a general-population reason to supplement.]

Citation. MRC Vitamin Study Research Group. Lancet 1991;338:131–137. SACN. Vitamin D and Health 2016. NICE PH56 Vitamin D. BSH. Br J Haematol 2014. Stoffel NU et al. Lancet Haematol 2017;4(11):e524–e533. SACN. Iron and Health 2010.

The structural critique

Supplement the molecule vs supplement the engineered claim vehicle.

[Prose to draft, ~250–400 words: the structural-pattern reading. The evidence base on named nutrients in deficiency-targeted populations is strong. The product environment selling ‘optimisation’ to non-deficient adults runs on three structural moves: (a) authorised general-function claims from EU/GB Article 13.1 used as marketing copy with quiet caveats; (b) borderline-product presentation creeping toward implicit disease-risk-reduction (the MHRA presentation-test territory); (c) bundled multi-ingredient products where individual-nutrient RCT evidence cannot transfer to the bundle. The manufacturer is creator of the gap — sourcing the ingredients, engineering the claim language to stay within authorised territory, marketing to wellness-aspirant adults, commissioning the favourable observational research (see Industry Funding Bias). The molecule-vs-vehicle distinction from Caffeine and Health applies: the molecule is dose-dependent and named-substance-studied; the vehicle is formulated and marketed beyond what the molecule evidence supports.]

How to read the table. The % NRV figure is a label-disclosure rule under Food Supplements (England) Regulations 2003 SI 2003/1387 Schedule, set against Annex XIII NRVs. It is not a safety ceiling. The safety ceiling for chronic intake is the EFSA NDA Tolerable Upper Intake Level where one has been set — and the EFSA UL applies to total intake from all sources: supplement + fortified food + diet + sun exposure for vitamin D. The two scales are not equivalent. The high-potency vitamin D row above carries 2000% NRV on the label, which the consumer reads as ‘a lot’; what the label does not show is that the same dose lands at the EFSA UL ceiling from the supplement alone, before any fortified-food or sun-exposure contribution is added. The structural-pattern critique is that the label-disclosure rule does not surface the safety-ceiling distance, and the consumer carries the gap.

High-risk groups

Seven populations for whom the population-average reading does not apply.

Pregnancy and pre-conception.

400 µg/day folic acid pre-conception through 12 weeks; 10 µg/day vitamin D; avoidance of retinol (vitamin A) megadosing for teratogenic risk; herbal-product caveats (St John's Wort, raspberry leaf, blue cohosh); the high-dose caffeine cross-reference for combined-product supplements. NICE NG201 antenatal care; RCOG guidance; NHS Start4Life. Source-to-evidence at v1.0 draft.

Infants, children, and adolescents.

Healthy Start vitamin drops scheme; iron-deficiency in toddlers (SACN 2010); caffeine 3 mg/kg/day EFSA ceiling cross-link to Caffeine and Health; ASA / CAP rules on under-16 marketing of health-related products; the influencer-marketing exposure dimension (Boyland 2016 AJCN). The structural-pattern critique applies most acutely here — products are formulated and marketed at adolescents with the highest claim density and the weakest paediatric evidence base.

Older adults and polypharmacy patients.

B12 absorption decline with age (BSH 2014); vitamin D deficiency prevalence; the polypharmacy interaction surface — St John's Wort + SSRIs / contraceptives, vitamin K + warfarin, calcium + thiazide diuretics, high-dose vitamin E + warfarin, statin + grapefruit-extract products. NHS Yellow Card under-reporting is the live evidence-base limitation.

Vegan, vegetarian, and culturally-restricted-diet populations.

B12 deficiency risk and the cleanest supplementation case (BSH 2014); iron bioavailability (non-haem); iodine deficiency in the dairy-substitute transition (Bath 2017 BJN); long-chain omega-3 algal sources for vegetarians; cross-link to Cultural Food Myths.

Ethnic-minority populations with elevated deficiency prevalence.

Vitamin D deficiency in South Asian and Black African / Caribbean populations (Cashman 2016 AJCN; SACN 2016); the institutional-pathway-access equity dimension (Adams 2016 PLOS Med); within-community variation as a discipline; community-specific dietitian routing as the institutional pathway, not the consumer supplement.

Hypertensive, diabetic, and renal-impaired populations.

Potassium / magnesium supplementation risk in eGFR-impaired patients; calcium and CVD signal (Bolland 2010 BMJ — contested); vitamin E and warfarin; NICE NG203 chronic kidney disease; the clinical-supervision-required register. Population-supplementation reasoning does not transfer to clinical-condition-managed patients.

Athletes and military / occupational populations.

WADA prohibited-list contamination risk (IOC 2018 BJSM Maughan); Informed Sport / NSF Certified for Sport third-party certification; the elite-athlete evidence base sold to the recreational user as the structural-pattern translation gap; the protein-supplement vs resistance-training paired-evidence frame (Morton 2018 BJSM).

Conflicts and uncertainties

Four live tensions in the literature.

Naming them strengthens the evidence base; hiding them weakens it.

1. Voluntary vs mandatory folic acid flour fortification.

[Prose to draft for v1.0: UK 2021–2024 decision process; the public-health-effectiveness-vs-individual-choice tension; the masking-of-B12-deficiency argument and its empirical strength; the cross-jurisdictional comparison with US (mandatory since 1998) and the NTD-prevalence-reduction estimates.]

2. Observational vs RCT divergence in the multivitamin evidence base.

[Prose to draft for v1.0: a textbook case of observational signal not surviving randomisation. Nutritional epidemiology cohorts consistently show inverse associations between supplement use and various endpoints. RCTs (USPSTF 2022 evidence base; COSMOS; VITAL; Bjelakovic Cochrane) close most of those associations. The structural-pattern reading: supplement users self-select on socioeconomic, lifestyle, and health-engagement variables that the observational adjustment cannot fully neutralise.]

3. Borderline-product classification creep.

[Prose to draft for v1.0: herbal and wellness products marketed with implicit disease-risk-reduction claims approaching medicines-presentation territory. ASA / CMA enforcement caseload through 2025–2026 (cross-link to SCANSMART External Scan ledger). The MHRA function-test vs presentation-test boundary — the structural-pattern question is whether the enforcement environment can scale with the marketing-claim volume.]

4. Yellow Card under-reporting for supplements.

[Prose to draft for v1.0: adverse events linked to food supplements are reportable to the MHRA Yellow Card scheme but under-reported relative to medicines; the published evidence base on harm signals is therefore weaker than the apparent safety case would suggest. The honest reading: the precautionary case for caution at high-dose chronic supplementation is stronger than the published-harm tally alone implies.]

What this brief does not claim.
