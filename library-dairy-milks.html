Dairy Milks — Evidence Vault.

Dairy milk is the most regulated, most subsidised, most institutionally embedded beverage in the UK diet — and the one whose nutritional guidance is currently the least settled. SACN's 2019 Saturated Fats and Health re-affirmed the 10% population cap on saturated fatty acids, recommending lower-fat dairy switching as the default. The PURE study (Dehghan 2018 Lancet, n=136,384) found whole-fat dairy associated with a 29% lower composite cardiovascular endpoint at higher consumption; the dairy-matrix hypothesis (Astrup 2019) argues the fatty-acid-isolation model on which the SACN cap rests does not capture how dairy lipids behave inside their native phospholipid-protein matrix. The A2 marketing claim — that milk containing only A2 β-casein is "easier to digest" — has been the subject of an ASA upheld ruling against Nutricia (2022) and an EFSA literature review (2009) finding no causal relationship between A1 β-casein and non-communicable disease. Raw drinking milk sales remain legal in England, Wales and Northern Ireland direct from producer (≈0.01% of UK cows' milk volume) and prohibited for drinking in Scotland; the FSA estimates the risk profile remains a small but real public-health concern. Lactose-free milk is a £-premium category that solves a real condition for some adults (5–15% Northern European; 60–80% South Asian and African heritage; 70–100% East Asian heritage lactase non-persistence) and is mis-purchased by many for whom it confers no benefit. This brief decodes eight dairy-milk categories — whole, semi-skimmed, 1%, skimmed, organic, A2, raw, lactose-free — against UK regulatory architecture, the SACN guidance versus the dairy-matrix re-evaluation, school and nursery milk subsidy infrastructure, A2 marketing-claim governance, and raw-milk public-health risk.

⭐ The headline finding

The 10% cap, the PURE study, and the dairy matrix.

The dominant UK public-health message on dairy — "switch to lower-fat versions" — sits on a 10%-of-energy population cap on saturated fatty acid (SFA) intake first set by COMA in 1994 and re-affirmed by SACN in its 2019 systematic review Saturated Fats and Health. The review examined 47 systematic reviews and meta-analyses; concluded the relationship between SFA intake and serum LDL-cholesterol is well established; concluded the relationship between SFA intake and incident cardiovascular disease remains "best summarised" by the 10% cap; and recommended population-level SFA reduction primarily by replacement with unsaturated fats from non-tropical plant oils, with lower-fat dairy switching cited as the most accessible practical move (milk and milk products supply 20–30% of UK dietary SFA depending on age band per NDNS).

What SACN 2019 explicitly did not do, and what the dairy-matrix literature has since framed as a methodological gap: it did not separate the effects of dairy-SFA-consumed-in-dairy from the effects of dairy-SFA-consumed-as-an-isolated-fatty-acid. The Dehghan 2018 Lancet PURE study (n=136,384, 21 countries, 9.1-year mean follow-up) found a higher dairy intake (≥ 2 servings/day vs none) associated with a 17% lower all-cause mortality and a 16% lower composite cardiovascular outcome; whole-fat dairy consumers showed a 29% lower composite endpoint vs non-consumers, with no signal that the saturated-fat content of whole milk degraded the dairy-cardiovascular association. A 2019 European Heart Journal state-of-the-art review (Astrup et al.) and a 2020 Federation of European Nutrition Societies consensus paper articulated the dairy-matrix hypothesis: that the milk fat globule membrane (MFGM), the calcium-fatty-acid soap-forming pathway, the phospholipid-encapsulation of SFA, and the protein-bound calcium are jointly responsible for dairy lipids behaving non-equivalently to free SFA on cardiometabolic outcomes.

The honest reading. SACN's 10% cap is correctly read as a guidance-level population-cap recommendation anchored on LDL-cholesterol as an intermediate biomarker. The observational evidence on dairy and cardiovascular disease is not a consistent positive signal at the food-group level for whole-fat milk; multiple cohort studies and the PURE prospective programme show null-to-protective associations, not the harm signal the 10% cap implies if read literally as a per-food rule. The point is not that SACN was wrong. The point is that the population guidance translates poorly into a per-food shopping rule. Lower-fat dairy switching is reasonable as an SFA-reduction lever where total dietary SFA exceeds 10% of energy; it is not reasonable as a categorical "whole milk is unhealthy" claim. The equity dimension follows Adams 2016 PLOS Med: messages that translate population guidance into product-level avoidance disproportionately affect households where whole milk is the cheapest, most calorie-dense, and most cuisine-relevant source of dietary calcium, protein and fat for children and pensioners.

The structural critique. The dairy milk category is a case study in how a defensible population-level public-health cap becomes, at the supermarket shelf, a moralised product-level binary (red top vs blue top, with green now as the "compromise") that misallocates attention from the products where SFA reduction would matter most — pastry, fried food, ultra-processed meat and confectionery — to a category whose food-matrix biology operates differently from the isolated-fatty-acid model on which the cap is built.

Foundational framework

What "milk" is, legally.

In UK retained law, milk is the regular mammary secretion obtained from one or more milkings, without anything being added or removed (EU 1308/2013 Annex VII Part III, retained post-Brexit). The compositional standards: minimum protein 3.0% w/w, minimum fat content varies by category. "Cow's milk" sold for direct human consumption in the UK is by default pasteurised — heat treatment per Annex III, Section IX, Regulation (EC) 853/2004 — and is categorised at retail by its fat content. The CJEU C-422/16 Tofu Town decision (14 June 2017) reserved the name "milk" for mammary secretion under EU/UK retained law; this is the boundary that produces the plant-drink naming-discipline rule (oat drink, almond drink, soya drink — see Plant Milks). The dairy category sits inside the reserved descriptor.

UK retail dairy milk falls in four standardised fat categories. The cap colour convention is industry convention, not law — though universally consistent enough across major UK processors (Arla, Müller, Yeo Valley, Cooperative-branded supplies) to function as a de facto consumer cue.

Two structural readings of the table that matter at the shelf. Protein, calcium, iodine, and B12 are essentially identical across the four fat categories; the only meaningful differences are calorie and fat content. The micronutrient-density argument for whole milk over skimmed for children is weak; the dietary-fat-as-vehicle-for-fat-soluble-vitamins argument is also weaker than commonly assumed, since the small amount of vitamin A and D added back to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by UK fortification (and by natural cow's milk content for whole) leaves all four categories at broadly similar fat-soluble-vitamin status.

The dairy fat matrix.

Cow's milk fat is approximately 65% saturated, 30% monounsaturated, and 5% polyunsaturated, with the SFA profile dominated by palmitic (C16:0), stearic (C18:0), and myristic (C14:0). The fat sits inside the milk fat globule membrane (MFGM) — a phospholipid bilayer containing sphingomyelin, cholesterol, glycoproteins, and gangliosides — and inside a colloidal calcium-phosphate-casein matrix that binds dietary calcium to the SFA molecules. Calcium-fatty-acid soap formation in the gut reduces the absorption of palmitic acid by 5–10% relative to its free-acid bioavailability (Lorenzen 2014 Br J Nutr); MFGM phospholipids appear to alter post-prandial lipaemia in ways that favour reverse cholesterol transport (Demmer 2016 J Nutr Sci).

The dairy-matrix hypothesis (Astrup et al. 2019; Mozaffarian & Wu 2018) makes a specific structural claim: that fatty-acid-by-fatty-acid analysis of dairy SFA effects misrepresents what happens when SFA is consumed inside its native dairy matrix. The hypothesis is not unanimously accepted; SACN 2019 explicitly considered and declined to incorporate the dairy-matrix argument as a basis for revising the 10% population cap, on the basis that the LDL-cholesterol response to dairy SFA is consistent across food-matrix forms and that LDL-cholesterol remains the load-bearing intermediate biomarker for population guidance.

The reading that best fits the evidence: the dairy-matrix hypothesis is well-supported for the relationship between dairy-SFA-and-cardiovascular-outcomes, less well-supported for the relationship between dairy-SFA-and-LDL-cholesterol. Different intermediate biomarkers point in different directions. The guidance-level cap is calibrated to LDL; the cardiovascular-outcome-level evidence is the basis for not extending the cap into a categorical anti-whole-milk claim.

🗺️ The dairy milk category map

Eight categories on the UK shelf.

1. Whole milk (3.5% fat, blue cap).

The full-fat version. ~65 kcal/100 ml. SACN 2019 recommends switching out where SFA intake exceeds 10% of energy. NDNS data (combined Years 9–11, published 2023) shows UK adult mean SFA intake at 12.5% of food energy — above the cap. Whole milk is the standardised default for children under 2 per NHS Start4Life (full-fat is preferred to semi-skimmed under-2 because the calorie density supports growth velocity); 2–5 year-olds may switch to semi-skimmed if growth is on track and family diet is otherwise adequate; under-5 nursery provision uses whole or semi-skimmed at the setting's discretion under the Nursery Milk Scheme. Whole milk also functions as the default in many South Asian, Mediterranean, Caribbean and African diaspora households where the cuisine architecture is built around it (chai-bases, yoghurt and paneer culture, cooked-milk dishes, breakfast porridge). The categorical "switch from whole" message reads differently in those contexts than in a household where the calorie-and-fat replacement is a butter-on-toast breakfast.

2. Semi-skimmed milk (1.5–1.8% fat, green cap).

The mainstream UK retail default; ~47 kcal/100 ml; SFA roughly halved from whole. The most-purchased UK milk category by volume (AHDB Dairy market reports). Sits within the SACN guidance space without requiring further switching for most adults. The micronutrient profile is essentially unchanged from whole. The flavour-perceptibility difference from whole is moderate; the difference from 1% is small; the difference from skimmed is substantial. Semi-skimmed has functioned as the institutional default for UK schools, hospitals, and care homes under the Government Buying Standards for Food (GBSF).

3. 1% milk (~1.0% fat, varied cap colour).

UK retail innovation, mainstream since the early 2010s. Sits between semi-skimmed and skimmed on SFA content but closer to semi-skimmed on palatability. Retailers position it as an SFA-reduction lever that does not require accepting skimmed-milk mouthfeel. The 1% category does not have its own legal compositional definition; it is sold against the "partially skimmed milk" technical category under retained Annex VII.

4. Skimmed milk (< 0.3% fat, red cap).

The lowest-fat category. ~33 kcal/100 ml; SFA essentially eliminated. Skimmed milk loses none of its protein, calcium, iodine, or B12; the loss is calorie density, fat-soluble-vitamin baseline (offset for UK production by fortification of vitamin A and D back into skimmed and semi-skimmed for retail-sold product), and the food-matrix effects on satiety and post-prandial lipaemia. Generally not recommended as the default for children under 5 (DHSC guidance: under-2 whole milk; 2–5 semi-skimmed acceptable; over-5 skimmed acceptable if growth is on track). The category's volume share has declined as 1% has expanded.

5. Organic milk.

Defined under retained EU Regulation 2018/848, UK organic certification under the Soil Association, OF&G Organic, Organic Farmers & Growers, Quality Welsh Food Certification, and Biodynamic Association Certification standards. Organic dairy production requires cattle on minimum grazing time outdoors (typically 200 days/year), prohibits routine antibiotic use, prohibits genetically-modified feed, and requires a higher forage component of feed than conventional dairy. The compositional difference: organic milk has been documented to contain 56% more total n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids than conventional milk (Średnicka-Tober et al. 2016 Br J Nutr); 41% more conjugated linoleic acid (CLA); 25% lower iodine; lower selenium. The omega-3 and CLA differences are statistically robust but small in absolute terms — most adults need to consume more than a litre of organic milk per day for the omega-3 difference to make a clinically-meaningful contribution to dietary intake. The iodine reduction is the more consequential difference: NHS guidance routinely recommends milk as an iodine source for pregnant and lactating women, and substituting organic milk lowers per-litre iodine input. Cost premium: organic milk retails at approximately 30–50% above conventional semi-skimmed at UK 2026 prices.

6. A2 milk (β-casein A2-only or A2-dominant).

A commercial category defined by the protein composition: standard cow's milk contains a mixture of A1 and A2 variants of β-casein, with the A1 variant predominant in many Holstein-Friesian herds and the A2 variant exclusive in some heritage breeds (Jersey, Guernsey, Brown Swiss; and selected Holstein lines bred for A2-homozygous status). The A2 Milk Company brought the category to UK retail 2012; it withdrew from the UK market in 2019 (specialty re-stocking by smaller producers has continued, but the major brand is no longer present at scale). The marketing claim is that A1 β-casein, on digestion, releases β-casomorphin-7 (BCM-7), an opioid peptide hypothesised to contribute to GI discomfort, type-1 diabetes risk, and (variably) other conditions; A2 milk, lacking A1 β-casein, avoids the BCM-7 release.

The evidence. EFSA's 2009 review of the BCM-7-and-non-communicable-disease literature concluded that there was insufficient evidence to support a causal relationship between dietary intake of BCM-7 and type-1 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, autism, or any other named condition. A small number of short-duration trials in adults with self-reported GI discomfort on conventional milk have reported reduced symptoms on A2-only milk (Jianqin 2016; He 2017); the trials are small, industry-funded in significant part, and have not been replicated in independent larger trials. A 2022 ASA upheld ruling against Nutricia (G21-1111826) for the claim that A2-protein milk "may be easier to digest than regular cow's milk" found the claim breached the CAP Code on the basis that no authorised EU/UK health claim covers the A2-protein-and-digestibility association.

The honest reading. A2 milk is a marketed-protein-difference category. Some individuals report subjective GI benefit; the population-level evidence is weak; the regulatory authority has explicitly ruled that the "easier to digest" claim is not authorised. The category is appropriately read as a consumer-choice product, not as a clinically-justified nutritional category.

7. Raw (unpasteurised) drinking milk.

Cow's milk that has not been heat-treated. Regulated under the Food Safety and Hygiene (England) Regulations 2013 (and equivalent in Wales and Northern Ireland) and under retained EU 853/2004. In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: raw drinking milk (RDM) may be sold by registered producers direct from the farm gate, at farmers' markets, on milk rounds, and as part of a farm's catering operation — but not through retail supply chains. Producers must be registered with the FSA, must source from herds tested free of bovine TB and brucellosis, must label the product with the FSA-prescribed health warning ("This milk has not been heat-treated and may therefore contain organisms harmful to health"), and must meet hygiene-management requirements with sampling and microbiological testing. In Scotland: sale of raw drinking milk for human consumption is prohibited under the Food Standards Scotland Regulations (the ban dates to 1983 and has been retained). Raw drinking milk accounts for an estimated 0.01% of UK cows' milk volume.

The risk profile. Raw milk may contain Campylobacter, Salmonella, Listeria, E. coli O157 (STEC), and Brucella (where TB-and-brucellosis surveillance fails). UK outbreak data: periodic STEC O157 outbreak clusters traced to raw-milk supply (UKHSA outbreak reports 2014, 2017, 2019, 2022 covering at least four distinct producers). The FSA's 2024–2025 review of the raw drinking milk regulatory framework retained the carve-out for direct-from-producer sale but tightened registration, hygiene, and labelling requirements. The risk-benefit framing: the claimed health benefits of raw milk (enhanced immunity, asthma prevention, improved digestion) are not supported by current peer-reviewed evidence at population-level effect-size; the documented infection risks are small in absolute terms (relative to total UK milk consumption) but disproportionate per-litre relative to pasteurised milk.

8. Lactose-free milk.

Cow's milk treated with the enzyme lactase (β-galactosidase) to hydrolyse lactose into its component monosaccharides (glucose and galactose). The product is nutritionally identical to standard milk on all macro- and micronutrients other than the lactose-glucose-galactose equilibrium — same protein, calcium, iodine, B12, fat (available in whole, semi-skimmed, or skimmed lactose-free variants). The flavour profile is slightly sweeter than standard milk due to the higher relative monosaccharide content. Lactose-free milk is the clinically-appropriate routing for lactose-intolerant individuals who wish to retain dairy in the diet — distinct from those with cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA), for whom lactose-free milk does not resolve the allergy (CMPA is a protein-level allergy, not a sugar-level intolerance). Cost premium: lactose-free milk retails approximately 30–50% above standard equivalents at UK 2026 prices.

The clinical population. Primary adult-onset lactase non-persistence is the genetic default; lactase persistence (continued lactase enzyme production into adulthood) is a human evolutionary adaptation present in approximately 95% of Northern European-ancestry adults, 60–80% of South Asian and African-heritage adults, and 0–30% of East Asian-ancestry adults (Itan 2009 PLOS Comp Biol; Liebert 2017 Hum Genet). The UK distribution by ethnicity follows the ancestry distribution; the population-level prevalence of clinical lactose intolerance in UK adults is approximately 5% (Northern European descent) to 70% (East Asian descent). Lactose-free milk is the appropriate routing for the clinically-affected population. It is not a nutritional upgrade for the lactase-persistent population; the category is sometimes purchased on a "premium" or "tolerated better" assumption that does not hold biochemically for individuals without lactase non-persistence.

The SACN 10% cap

What it is, what it isn't.

The cap is the longest-running specific dietary guidance in UK public health. Set by COMA's 1991 Dietary Reference Values for Food Energy and Nutrients, reset at the 10% threshold by COMA in 1994, re-affirmed by SACN's 2019 Saturated Fats and Health systematic review. The cap is calibrated to the LDL-cholesterol-response evidence base. The methodology: at the population level, SFA-substitution-with-PUFA reduces serum LDL-cholesterol; LDL-cholesterol is a causally-validated risk factor for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease; therefore population-level SFA reduction reduces population-level cardiovascular event rate.

What the cap covers. The 10% threshold is a food-energy-percentage recommendation — the proportion of total dietary energy contributed by saturated fatty acids. NDNS data: UK adults average approximately 12.5% (Years 9–11 combined); children 12.7%. The over-cap status is consistent across age bands.

What the cap does not do. The cap does not specify which food sources should bear the reduction. SACN 2019 cites unsaturated-vegetable-oil replacement as the load-bearing substitution; lower-fat-dairy switching is one route among many. The cap does not say "whole milk is harmful"; it says "if total dietary SFA exceeds 10%, the most likely sources contributing to over-cap status include dairy fat, meat fat, and (importantly) palm-and-coconut fat in processed food, butter on toast, and confectionery." Lower-fat dairy is the most-accessible-by-volume reduction lever; it is not the only lever and is not always the highest-yield lever per gram of SFA reduced.

The dairy-matrix re-evaluation. The peer-reviewed literature since 2015 has documented null-to-protective associations between full-fat dairy consumption and cardiovascular endpoints (Dehghan 2018 Lancet PURE; Drouin-Chartier 2016 Adv Nutr systematic review; de Oliveira Otto 2018 AJCN). The MFGM-and-calcium-soap mechanistic literature (Astrup 2019 Eur Heart J; Mozaffarian 2014 Annu Rev Nutr) provides a plausible mechanism for the non-equivalence. SACN 2019 considered the matrix literature, declined to revise the 10% cap on the basis that LDL-cholesterol remained the load-bearing biomarker. The disagreement between the SACN guidance and the dairy-matrix literature is methodological, not personal: SACN's role is to translate evidence into population-level public-health guidance using intermediate biomarkers where the long-term-outcome evidence is observational; the dairy-matrix literature's role is to identify and characterise food-matrix effects that may modify intermediate-biomarker translation.

The implication for FSMA Library framing. A guidance-level cap (SACN 10%) is a different epistemic object from a per-food rule. The guidance is correctly applied at the dietary-pattern level; it is mis-applied at the product-aisle level when read as a categorical "lower-fat is better" rule. The Eatwell Guide reflects the population-level reading: dairy-and-alternatives are a single sector of the guide, with the recommendation reading as "go for lower-fat where you have a choice" rather than "lower-fat is mandatory."

State-funded provision and institutional buying

The Nursery Milk Scheme, the School Milk schemes, and the GBSF.

Nursery Milk Scheme.

The Nursery Milk Scheme is a DHSC statutory entitlement, funded by central government via the Nursery Milk Reimbursement Unit (NMRU). It provides 189 ml (1/3 pint) of free milk per day to children under the age of five attending registered childcare for two or more hours. The scheme is worth approximately £49.3 million per year to English children (NMRU 2024–25 funding cycle data). Provision is restricted to whole or semi-skimmed pasteurised cow's milk for children over 12 months; powdered infant formula (cow's-milk-based) is provided for babies under 12 months. The scheme does not currently provide plant-drink alternatives for children with CMPA, lactose intolerance, or for families with vegan dietary preferences; this is a live policy debate (parliamentary petition 639254; Sustain campaign) but had not been implemented as of May 2026. The scheme is the largest single state-funded dairy-distribution programme in the UK.

School Milk Scheme.

England, Wales and Northern Ireland operated within the EU School Milk Scheme prior to Brexit; on exit, the EU scheme's UK allocation lapsed. Replacement provision in England is fragmented: the Cool Milk and Coolmilk-administered scheme provides subsidised milk to 5-and-overs in primary schools via DHSC and parental top-up; participation is at school discretion, not universal. Scotland operates the Scottish Milk and Healthy Snack Scheme for under-5s in registered childcare, with a separate Free School Milk for Primary Schools programme. Wales operates the Healthy Eating in Schools (Nutritional Standards) (Wales) Regulations 2013. Northern Ireland operates the NI School Milk Subsidy Scheme. The UK-wide picture is institutional but inconsistent.

Government Buying Standards for Food (GBSF).

The GBSF set procurement standards for food and drink purchased by central government departments and their agencies, schools, hospitals, prisons, and the armed forces. For dairy milk: GBSF specifies semi-skimmed as the default milk for school catering and central-government catering, with whole milk permitted for under-2s and skimmed milk available where requested. The Defra-led 2024 review of GBSF retained these thresholds; the 2025 update added sustainability scoring on dairy production (carbon-and-water footprint per litre, welfare-of-animal certification) as a tie-break criterion for procurement decisions. The GBSF threshold for milk is the institutional anchor for the "semi-skimmed as default" reading of UK dairy guidance — it operationalises the SACN 10% cap at the institutional-procurement level, where individual choice is constrained by central buying.

The cross-reading. UK dairy consumption in 2026 sits across three layers: state-funded provision (Nursery and School Milk) which is whole-or-semi-skimmed cow's milk by default; institutional buying (GBSF) which is semi-skimmed by default; household choice (retail), which spans the full category map. SACN's 10% cap is most directly operationalised at the GBSF institutional layer, indirectly at the Nursery and School Milk layer (which provides milk-as-calorie-source for the under-5 growth-velocity rationale, not as SFA-cap implementation), and least directly at the household-retail layer (where the cap reads as guidance, not procurement rule).

UK regulatory frame in detail

The composition, the hygiene carve-out, and the A2 ruling.

Compositional and labelling architecture.

UK dairy milk is regulated under a layered framework. Compositional standards sit under retained EU 1308/2013 Annex VII Part III, which specifies the legally-protected name "milk" for mammary secretion (the boundary the CJEU Tofu Town decision established for the plant-drink category to sit outside) and sets the four standard fat categories. Hygiene requirements sit under retained EU 853/2004 Annex III Section IX, which sets the pasteurisation requirement for retail-sold milk and the raw drinking milk carve-out for direct-from-producer sale. Labelling requirements sit under the Food Information Regulations 2014 and retained EU 1169/2011 — mandatory nutrition declaration; four-category fat-content labelling; lactose-free claim when applicable; organic claim when certified. Health and nutrition claims sit under retained EU 1924/2006 (UK Nutrition and Health Claims Regulation), which is the regulation that the ASA 2022 ruling against Nutricia operated under in finding the A2 "easier to digest" claim unauthorised.

The raw drinking milk regulatory architecture.

The most contested area of UK dairy regulation. The 2024–2025 FSA Board review considered whether to retain the carve-out, tighten it, or end it. The Board retained the carve-out on the basis that (a) the volumes involved are small (≈ 0.01% of UK cows' milk), (b) registered producers operate under tight microbiological-testing requirements, and (c) the carve-out preserves direct-from-farm sale as a small-producer income stream. The Board tightened registration and hygiene requirements — including STEC O157 surveillance protocols, mandatory annual sampling for Salmonella and Listeria, and prescribed-format labelling — but stopped short of the Scottish-style total ban.

The structural reading. The carve-out is defensible on small-producer-economic grounds and on consumer-choice grounds (the regulatory frame requires explicit labelling that informs the consumer about pasteurisation status). It is less defensible on pure public-health grounds: the residual risk profile is non-zero and disproportionate per-litre to pasteurised milk; periodic outbreak clusters traced to raw-milk supply continue. The trade-off is policy-judgement territory; the FSA's 2024–2025 decision was to keep the trade-off in favour of small-producer economy and consumer-choice with reinforced safety infrastructure, rather than to absorb the public-health risk through a total ban as Scotland did in 1983.

The A2 claim, the ASA ruling, and the NHC Register.

The A2 Milk Company's UK market presence (2012–2019) operated against the UK Nutrition and Health Claims Register, which is the regulatory list of pre-authorised health-and-nutrition claims that food businesses can make on labelling and in marketing. No claim relating to A2 β-casein and digestibility, gastrointestinal symptom relief, or any other clinical-or-health outcome is on the UK NHC Register. The 2022 Nutricia ruling (G21-1111826) by the Advertising Standards Authority specifically addressed an advertisement that included the claim "Initial studies on adults show that A2 protein milk may be easier to digest than regular cow's milk." The ASA found that the claim was not an authorised health claim under retained EU 1924/2006; that the supporting evidence (Jianqin 2016 in-vitro and small-trial data; He 2017 short-duration intervention) was not sufficient to establish the claim under the regulatory threshold; and that the advertisement breached the CAP Code on the basis. The ruling stands as the load-bearing UK regulatory position on A2 milk claims.

The implication for the FSMA framing. The A2 category exists as a marketed-product category. Individual consumers may report subjective tolerance benefit; the regulatory authority has explicitly ruled that the "easier to digest" claim cannot be made in UK marketing. The Library framing treats A2 milk as a consumer-choice category with a marketing-claim status that is currently unauthorised — distinct from lactose-free milk, which is a clinically-validated category for a genetically-defined population.

The SDIL 2028 reform and the milk-based-drinks exemption.

The Soft Drinks Industry Levy (SDIL) was introduced 2018 with a milk-based-drinks exemption (drinks containing ≥ 75% milk by volume are exempt from levy) and a plant-drink exemption (containing ≥ 120 mg calcium / 100 ml are exempt). The November 2025 HM Treasury / HMRC consultation outcome announced the proposed removal of both exemptions from 1 January 2028, with a parallel "intrinsic sugars" carve-out (lactose in milk-based drinks; the "core" plant ingredient in plant drinks) to mirror the existing 100% fruit juice exemption. As of May 2026, implementing regulations have not been published; the 2028 reform is announced policy intention, not finalised law. This is the same calibration applied in the Fruit Juice v1.1 and Plant Milks v1.1 briefs. The implication for dairy: flavoured milk drinks, milkshakes, and yoghurt-type products that fall under the milk-based-drinks exemption would, if the 2028 reform proceeds as announced, become subject to the levy on their added sugars. Unmodified standardised dairy milk (whole, semi-skimmed, 1%, skimmed) is not affected; the change concerns the category boundary between dairy-as-base and dairy-as-flavoured-drink (the latter is the substrate of the forthcoming Children's Milk Drinks and Flavoured Milks sibling piece).

International precedent

How the UK frame compares.

United States. FDA 21 CFR 131.110 standardises cow's milk for fat content; the US permits "milk" terminology for plant drinks (FDA 2023 draft guidance), creating the UK/EU vs US naming-discipline divergence. The US Grade A Pasteurized Milk Ordinance is the equivalent regulatory architecture to UK 853/2004; the US permits state-level raw milk regulation, leading to a patchwork map (some states permit retail raw milk; some permit cow-share and herd-share models; some prohibit sale entirely). The CDC's surveillance of raw-milk-associated outbreaks is the international comparator for the FSA's UK raw-milk surveillance.

European Union. EU Regulation 1308/2013 Annex VII Part III is the parent regulation for the retained UK framework. EU 853/2004 governs heat treatment. EU member states operate national variations of the raw drinking milk carve-out: France permits raw milk retail in specialty cheese-and-dairy outlets; Germany permits Vorzugsmilch (preferred milk) under tight microbiological standards; Italy permits raw milk vending machines at registered farm gates. The EU School Milk Scheme provided subsidised milk to under-12s in member states; the UK exited the scheme on Brexit (the UK allocation lapsed in 2021 and was not directly replaced).

Canada. Federal prohibition of raw milk sale for human consumption since 1991 (Food and Drug Regulations Section B.08.002.2); provincial enforcement. The Canadian dairy supply management system (quotas; farm-gate price stabilisation; tariff-rate-quota protection from US imports) is the structural-difference comparator with UK dairy production.

Australia and New Zealand. Federal prohibition of raw milk sale for human consumption under Standard 1.2.10 of the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code. The "bath milk" carve-out (sold for cosmetic use; widely understood as a route for consumer purchase) was tightened after a 2014 child death from raw-milk consumption.

WHO and Codex Alimentarius. Codex MILK STANDARDS 206-1999 and 207-1999 set the international parent standards for cow's milk and milk products; the Codex Code of Hygienic Practice for Milk and Milk Products (CAC/RCP 57-2004) sets the international parent practice standards for pasteurisation and raw milk handling. WHO has no specific population-level recommendation on dairy SFA distinct from the broader saturated-fat-reduction framework; the WHO 2023 Saturated and Trans-Fatty Acid Intake guideline aligns broadly with the SACN 10% cap.

Ireland. The Sugar Sweetened Drinks Tax (introduced 2018) has no milk-based-drinks exemption equivalent to UK SDIL; this is a relevant comparator if the UK proceeds with the 2028 reform of the milk-based-drinks exemption.

High-risk groups

Seven populations for whom the population-average reading does not apply.

1. Children under 2 — the whole-milk requirement.
NHS Start4Life and BDA paediatric guidance: full-fat (whole) milk is the standardised default for children 1–2 years of age, on the basis that calorie density supports growth velocity and that fat-soluble vitamin absorption is supported by the higher fat content. Semi-skimmed is acceptable from age 2 if growth is on track and dietary diversity is adequate. Skimmed is generally not recommended until age 5 or older. The Nursery Milk Scheme provides whole or semi-skimmed by default. The under-2 population is the case where the "lower-fat is better" SACN-cap reading is most clearly inappropriate — the calorie and fat-density requirement of early childhood overrides the population-level SFA cap.
2. Children aged 2–5 — the transitional cohort.
Semi-skimmed acceptable from age 2 if growth is on track and family diet is adequate. School Milk and Nursery Milk Scheme provision uses semi-skimmed by default for the older end of the under-5 band. The cohort sits at the boundary between the under-2 whole-milk default and the over-5 broader-choice space. Family decisions in this band are appropriately led by a HV / GP / RD where there is a growth-velocity or growth-faltering concern; the default in absence of such concern is semi-skimmed.
3. South Asian and African heritage adults — the lactase non-persistence band.
Adult lactase non-persistence affects approximately 60–80% of UK adults of South Asian and African heritage (Itan 2009 PLOS Comp Biol). Clinical-routing options: lactose-free milk; cultured dairy where lactose is fermented to lactic acid (yoghurt, kefir, some hard cheeses); fortified plant drink alternatives; community-cuisine-specific routings (ghee in South Asian cuisine, where the fat-soluble vitamin component is retained while the lactose component is essentially removed; akpan and sorghum-based fermented drinks in some West African traditions). The structural reading: the UK dairy-default infrastructure (Nursery Milk Scheme; School Milk; GBSF) provides standard pasteurised cow's milk without lactose-free or fermented alternatives, which is the equity-of-provision gap.
4. East Asian heritage adults — the highest-prevalence non-persistence band.
70–100% adult lactase non-persistence in UK adults of East Asian heritage. The same clinical-routing options apply, with the practical observation that East Asian traditional cuisine has substantially less integrated cow's milk than South Asian or African; the dietary-pattern transition that follows migration into UK dairy infrastructure is a separate substrate.
5. Cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA) — paediatric population.
CMPA affects approximately 1.0–7.5% of UK infants under 2 years of age (BSACI 2024 estimates; iMAP guidelines). The clinical routing is extensively-hydrolysed formula or amino-acid formula for under-12-month-olds; specialist-led routing to soy or oat plant drinks for over-12-month-olds (per BSACI guidance and the Plant Milks v1.1 sibling brief). Lactose-free milk does not resolve CMPA, which is a protein-level allergy (β-lactoglobulin and α-casein are the load-bearing allergens) distinct from lactose intolerance. The Nursery Milk Scheme does not currently provide plant-drink alternatives for CMPA-affected children; this is the active policy debate.
6. Pregnant and lactating women — the iodine dimension.
UK pregnant women are routinely deficient in iodine intake at the population level (Bath 2013 Lancet; Bath 2017); dairy milk is the largest dietary source of iodine for most UK women (≈ 30 µg/100 ml in conventional milk; ≈ 22 µg/100 ml in organic milk per Średnicka-Tober 2016). Switching from conventional dairy to organic reduces per-litre iodine input by approximately 25%; switching from dairy to fortified plant drinks reduces it further (only 34% of UK non-organic plant drinks are iodine-fortified; near-zero for organic per Clegg 2023). For pregnant women, the iodine intake is consequential: mild-to-moderate maternal iodine deficiency is associated with offspring neurodevelopment effects in the ALSPAC cohort (Bath 2013, n=1,040).
7. Older adults at risk of sarcopenia and osteoporosis.
Adults over 65 face age-related sarcopenia (muscle mass loss) and osteoporosis (bone mineral density loss). Dairy protein (whey and casein; DIAAS 1.45 for cow's milk) is among the highest-quality dietary protein sources, with calcium and vitamin D content that supports bone mineral metabolism. The clinical guidance for older adults is typically 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day dietary protein (above the standard 0.8 g/kg/day for younger adults), with dairy as a useful contributor. The "lower-fat is better" SACN-cap reading translates differently in this cohort: in the cohort, the protein-and-calcium contribution of any milk fat-category outweighs the marginal SFA-reduction lever, and the choice between whole and lower-fat milk is appropriately led by personal lipid profile and personal cardiovascular risk profile.
Conflicts and uncertainties

Four live contradictions in the evidence and policy base.

1. The SACN 10% cap vs the dairy-matrix re-evaluation.

The largest live tension in UK dairy nutritional guidance. SACN's position is that LDL-cholesterol response remains the load-bearing intermediate biomarker; the dairy-matrix literature's position is that dairy-SFA-in-food-matrix-form behaves non-equivalently to isolated SFA on cardiovascular outcomes. The disagreement is not "SACN is wrong"; the disagreement is methodological — what intermediate biomarker should public health guidance be calibrated to, and how much weight should population guidance give to food-matrix effects that may modify the translation of LDL-cholesterol-response evidence into cardiovascular-outcome evidence. The honest reading: the 10% cap is well-justified as population-level dietary-pattern guidance; the categorical "whole milk is harmful" reading does not survive the food-matrix evidence; the practical implication is that lower-fat dairy switching is a reasonable SFA-reduction lever but is not the only lever and is not always the highest-yield lever per gram of SFA reduced.

2. The raw-milk carve-out — public health risk vs small-producer economy.

The FSA's retained position is that the residual public health risk of raw drinking milk sale (direct from producer, registered, labelled) is small enough at the volumes involved (≈ 0.01% of UK cows' milk) and the consumer-choice and small-producer-economic considerations are weighty enough that the carve-out is defensible. Scotland's 1983 prohibition reflects an opposite weighting. The honest reading: this is policy-judgement territory; both positions are defensible on different value-weightings; the FSA's 2024–2025 tightening of registration, surveillance and labelling requirements is a reasonable middle position; the residual risk is non-zero and acknowledged.

3. The A2 commercial category — marketed difference vs evidence.

The A2 milk category is marketed as a digestibility improvement. The ASA has ruled the "easier to digest" claim unauthorised under UK NHC. Some individual consumers report subjective tolerance benefit. The honest reading: the marketed-clinical-claim is not currently supported by UK regulatory authority; the consumer-choice category may exist for the population that reports subjective benefit; the structural critique is not that A2 milk is harmful but that the marketing has run ahead of the evidence base in a way that the ASA has corrected.

4. The Nursery Milk Scheme's plant-drink-alternatives gap.

The £49.3 m/year Nursery Milk Scheme provides cow's milk only. Children with CMPA, with diagnosed lactose intolerance, or in vegan families do not have a state-funded plant-drink alternative. This is the active live policy debate in the under-5 provision space (parliamentary petition 639254; Sustain campaign). The structural reading: this is an equity-of-provision gap whose resolution is a policy choice. The available evidence (BSACI iMAP; Plant Milks v1.1 fortification evidence) supports a clinically-appropriate routing if state-funded plant-drink provision is implemented; the policy choice has not yet been made as of May 2026.

Decoder moves

Fifteen specific reads, split by context.

At the shelf.

  1. The cap colour matters only as an SFA-reduction lever; it does not predict micronutrient density. Protein, calcium, iodine and B12 are essentially identical across whole, semi-skimmed, 1% and skimmed. The choice is calorie-and-fat dimension only.
  2. For children under 2, whole milk is the default; this is not a tradition or preference, it's growth-velocity guidance. Move to semi-skimmed from age 2 if growth is on track; the under-2 case is the clearest case where lower-fat-is-better reading is inappropriate.
  3. For pregnant and lactating women, the iodine dimension matters. Standard dairy provides ≈ 30 µg/100 ml iodine; organic dairy ≈ 22 µg/100 ml; fortified plant drinks 34% non-organic / near-zero organic. If switching off dairy in pregnancy, identify the iodine source the switch leaves you with.
  4. For lactose-intolerant individuals, lactose-free milk is the appropriate clinical routing. It is not a "premium" milk; it is the same milk with the lactose enzymatically hydrolysed. Cost premium is ~30–50%; clinical benefit is real for the affected population. Lactose-free milk does NOT address cow's milk protein allergy.
  5. Organic milk's nutritional case is omega-3-and-CLA-positive but iodine-negative. The omega-3 difference is real but small per litre; the iodine reduction is the more consequential trade-off for the load-bearing population (pregnancy; under-5s). The animal welfare and environmental case is the more defensible reason to choose organic, on current evidence.
  6. The A2 marketing claim "easier to digest" is not an authorised UK health claim. The ASA has explicitly ruled against the claim (Nutricia 2022). If A2 is purchased on perceived tolerance grounds and subjective benefit is reported, no clinical objection; if it is purchased on the basis of marketed clinical advantage, the regulatory position is that the advantage is not currently established.
  7. Raw drinking milk is legal direct-from-producer in England, Wales, and NI; sale is prohibited in Scotland. The labelling carries an explicit warning. The risk profile is small in absolute terms but disproportionate per-litre to pasteurised milk. The choice to buy raw is appropriately informed by the labelling.

At the kitchen.

  1. The PURE study finding on whole-milk-and-cardiovascular-outcomes is robust at the population level; SACN's 10% cap is the population-level guidance. The two read as compatible if the cap is read as dietary-pattern guidance (not per-product rule) and the PURE finding is read as evidence against the categorical "whole milk is harmful" claim (not evidence against SFA reduction broadly).
  2. For South Asian, African and East Asian heritage families, the lactose-tolerance question is real. The cooking-tradition routings (cultured dairy; fermented dairy; ghee; community-cuisine plant-based traditional drinks) work; the supermarket lactose-free routing also works; the assumption of universal cow's-milk-tolerance is a Northern-European-ancestry assumption.
  3. For under-5 settings (nursery, childminder, primary), the Nursery Milk and School Milk Scheme infrastructure is the default state-funded provision. Plant-drink alternatives are not currently funded; the route for CMPA or vegan families is private purchase. The petition / Sustain advocacy on this is live; the policy is not yet changed.

At the household and cultural dimension.

  1. In households where whole milk is the calorie-dense cuisine anchor (cooked-milk dishes; chai; porridge; traditional yoghurt cultures), the SACN cap reading is appropriately weighted against the cultural and equity dimensions. Categorical anti-whole-milk messaging in these contexts is the messaging-form Adams 2016 PLOS Med argues against on equity grounds.
  2. For older adults at risk of sarcopenia or osteoporosis, dairy protein is among the highest-quality dietary protein sources. The dairy-protein dimension is part of the rationale for not over-reading the SACN cap into a per-product rule for older adults.
  3. The cheese-and-yoghurt category is a separate substrate (covered in forthcoming FSMA pieces) where the fermentation, the ripening, the calcium-and-protein-density, and the dairy-matrix effects further modify the SFA-isolation reading; this brief covers liquid drinking milk only.
  4. Where dietary diversity is constrained by income, dairy milk is the cheapest per-gram calcium and per-gram quality-protein source available at UK retail. The cost-and-equity dimension is the structural-justice argument against over-reading the SACN cap into a "lower fat is better" rule that disproportionately disadvantages low-income households where the calorie-and-fat-density of whole milk is a meaningful nutritional asset.
  5. The dairy-environmental case is real and well-documented (Poore & Nemecek 2018 Science: 9 kg CO₂-eq/L for cow's milk; 628 L water; 9 m² land). Where environmental footprint is a load-bearing decision factor, the routing into fortified plant drinks (oat or soy preferred over almond on water-scarcity grounds) is appropriate; the within-family clinical-routing (iodine, protein quality, BSACI safety for paediatric CMPA) should be checked against the Plant Milks v1.1 brief.

Defamation-safety statement

What this brief does not claim.

This brief does not allege unlawful conduct by any named party. It does not allege that named dairy producers, retailers, or food businesses have breached UK food safety, labelling, hygiene, or advertising regulation. Discussion of industry practice — including the historical A2 Milk Company market presence (2012–2019), the Nutricia 2022 ASA-upheld ruling, the AHDB Dairy market reporting, and the general dairy-industry environmental footprint per Poore & Nemecek 2018 — is supported by named peer-reviewed primary sources, named regulatory authority rulings, and named institutional publications cited inline.

Named-party reference policy. Every named company, brand, product, or corporate transaction in this brief is sourced to one of the following public-record categories: (a) published Companies House filings; (b) annual reports and segmental reporting; (c) financial-press coverage of publicly-disclosed transactions; (d) the party's own publicly-disclosed reformulation or product announcements; (e) peer-reviewed evaluation literature naming the party; (f) regulatory authority rulings published by the ASA, the FSA, the CMA, the MHRA, or the equivalent UK or international authority; (g) UK / EU / international government tracking and surveillance reports. No factual claim is made beyond what named parties have placed in the public record or what has been published in contemporaneous peer-reviewed evaluation.

Defamation safety throughout. Named-party references in this brief follow public-record-only and peer-reviewed-evaluation-only sourcing from the moment they appear. The ASA ruling against Nutricia is referenced as the public-record ruling it is, not as an allegation. The retained-EU regulatory framework and the FSA's 2024–2025 raw drinking milk review are referenced as published regulatory positions, not as critical commentary on individual producers.

Cultural-accuracy commitment. This brief touches diaspora-community food substrate (South Asian, African and East Asian heritage adult lactose tolerance distributions; cuisine architectures built around whole milk including chai, paneer, fermented cultured dairy, and traditional yoghurt cultures; community-specific traditional alternatives including ghee, akpan, sorghum-based fermented drinks and others). Every cuisine-specific claim is sourced to peer-reviewed work on the specific cuisine where peer-reviewed work exists; where the peer-reviewed work does not exist at the granular community level, the brief defers to BDA cultural-competence dietetic guidance and community-specific registered dietitians for individualised routing. Within-region and within-community variation in dietary patterns, lactose-tolerance distribution, and cuisine-architecture is substantial and named. The structural claim is that the UK dairy-default infrastructure (Nursery Milk Scheme; School Milk; GBSF) is calibrated to Northern-European-ancestry lactose-tolerance assumptions and that the equity-of-provision implications are real and named.

Educational-register positioning. SCANSMART is a food literacy and decision-support platform; it is not a medical device and does not provide medical advice. This brief sits at the educational layer. Specific clinical-dietary management should be guided by NICE-aligned clinical advice and registered-dietitian input; the structural critique of dairy-fat guidance, of the A2 marketing claim, and of the raw-milk regulatory carve-out is a structural-pattern claim about UK food architecture, not a clinical claim about specific products or individuals. The SACN 10% cap is correctly read as population-level public-health guidance; this brief argues against reading it as a per-product rule, not against the cap itself. The PURE study and the dairy-matrix literature are correctly read as food-matrix-effect evidence that modifies the SACN translation; this brief does not argue against SACN, it argues for accurate reading of the guidance against the food-matrix evidence base. Where individual clinical management is required — for paediatric CMPA, for diagnosed lactose intolerance, for pregnant women managing iodine status, for older adults managing sarcopenia and bone health — the routing is to a registered dietitian, the patient's GP, NICE-aligned clinical pathways, and the relevant BDA / BSACI / BSPGHAN clinical-society guidance.
