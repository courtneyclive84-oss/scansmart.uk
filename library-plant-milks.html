Plant Milks — Evidence Vault.

Plant milks are legally not "milk" in the EU and UK retained-law sense (Tofu Town ruling, 2017), are nutritionally not equivalent to cow's milk without fortification — and iodine is the load-bearing nutritional concern at the population level — and are environmentally generally lighter than dairy but with substantial within-category variation that the front-of-pack does not communicate. The brief decodes the legal name reservation, the iodine fortification gap, the wider micronutrient and protein quality questions, the soy-and-breast-cancer evidence, the environmental footprint, and the paediatric guidance.

⭐ The headline finding

The iodine gap, the name reservation, and the protein question.

Plant milks are legally not "milk." The EU's Common Markets in Agricultural Products Regulation (1308/2013) Annex VII Part III reserves the term "milk" for "the normal mammary secretion obtained from one or more milkings" and the term "milk products" for derivatives of that secretion. The Court of Justice of the European Union in Verband Sozialer Wettbewerb v TofuTown.com (Case C-422/16, judgment 14 June 2017) ruled that this reservation precludes the use of "milk," "butter," "cream," "cheese," "yoghurt" for purely plant-based products — even where qualified by clarifying terms like "soy milk" or "tofu butter" — unless the product is on the closed Annex I list of permitted exceptions. UK retains this position post-Brexit. UK plant-milk products are labelled "oat drink," "almond drink," "soya drink," not "oat milk" on the regulated front-of-pack.

Iodine is the load-bearing nutritional concern. Cow's milk is the largest single dietary source of iodine in the UK (NDNS Years 9–11 and 11–12 data). Plant-milk substitution without iodine fortification measurably reduces population iodine intake. Only 34% of non-organic UK plant milks are fortified with iodine (Clegg et al., 2023, International Journal of Food Sciences and Nutrition). The clinical stakes are highest in pregnancy: Bath et al. (Lancet, 2013, n = 1,040 UK pregnant women in the Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children) found that mild-to-moderate iodine deficiency during pregnancy was observationally associated with lower verbal IQ and reading scores in offspring at ages 8–9 years. The UK is iodine-replete-with-warning rather than iodine-replete-with-comfort.

The wider fortification picture. Clegg et al. 2023 UK market analysis: 92% of non-organic plant-based milk alternatives are fortified with at least one micronutrient — 87% with calcium, 79% with vitamin B12, 56% with vitamin B2 (riboflavin), and only 34% with iodine. Organic plant milks are typically not fortified — organic certification rules restrict synthetic vitamin additions; the proportion of organic plant drinks without B12 fortification approaches 100% in many product lines. A consumer choosing organic-plant-milk-as-substitute may be unknowingly receiving substantially less calcium, B12, and iodine than the cow's milk it replaces.

Protein quality and content differ substantially across plant milks. Cow's milk has a DIAAS of approximately 1.45 and a protein content of ~3.2 g/100ml; soy milk is the closest plant equivalent (DIAAS ~1.08, protein ~3.0 g/100ml); pea sits at DIAAS 0.8–0.9 with the highest protein content (~3.5 g/100ml); coconut at DIAAS 0.72; oat at DIAAS 0.59 (~1.0 g/100ml); rice at DIAAS 0.43 (~0.1 g/100ml); almond at DIAAS 0.39 (~0.5 g/100ml). The British Society for Allergy and Clinical Immunology (BSACI) routes paediatric cow's milk allergy management through soy, pea, and oat as preferred options for children over 12 months and lists almond and cashew as least preferred, primarily because of the protein-quality and content gap. A critical safety note: for infants under 6 months with CMPA, soy is itself a major allergen with approximately 10–14% cross-reactivity to cow's milk protein; introduction in this age group requires paediatric dietetic supervision and is not a shelf-side-substitution decision.

Environmentally, plant milks are generally lighter than dairy — with substantial within-category variation. Poore & Nemecek (Science, 2018), the largest food-system life-cycle assessment in the published literature (≈38,700 farms, 119 countries, 40 agricultural goods), found that producing 1 litre of cow's milk requires approximately 9 kg CO₂-eq greenhouse-gas emissions, ~9 m² of land, and ~628 litres of water (scarcity-weighted). Plant-milk GHG emissions are substantially lower (oat ~0.9, soy ~1.0, almond ~0.7 kg CO₂-eq/L), land use is substantially lower, water use is lower-on-average — with the major exception of almond, where the global weighted-average water footprint is approximately 371 L/L (California-sourced ~500–600 L/L due to irrigation intensity and water scarcity in the Central Valley).

Soy and breast cancer — the most contested historical claim, currently resolved in the evidence base. Consolidated in multiple meta-analyses (Wei et al. 2020; Messina 2016; WCRF/AICR CUP 2018 updated 2023): soy isoflavone intake at levels consistent with traditional Asian dietary patterns is observationally associated with reduced breast cancer risk in both pre-menopausal and post-menopausal women, and is not contraindicated in breast cancer survivors. The brief disavows the "soy causes hormone problems" framing that persists in lay commentary. For those taking levothyroxine for hypothyroidism, separating soy intake by 4 hours is sufficient; no contraindication at dietary doses.

The single most practical decoder on the page: check the fortification panel for calcium ≥ 120 mg/100ml, iodine specifically, and B12, before treating any plant milk as a cow's milk substitute. Iodine first. The choice within the category matters more than the choice between the category and dairy.

The shelf taxonomy

Seven plant-base products, not one category.

Every substantive claim later in the brief depends on which of the seven plant-base products is being discussed.

(a) Oat. Made from oat groats + water, often with a small fraction of canola or sunflower oil, salt, and stabilisers. Calcium-fortified (typically 120 mg/100ml in UK brands). Naturally contains soluble fibre (beta-glucan) and a moderate glycaemic load. Protein ~1.0 g/100ml; DIAAS ~0.59. Highest UK market share among plant milks as of 2024. Barista variants add stabilisers (commonly dipotassium phosphate or gellan gum) to allow steaming.

(b) Soy. Made from soaked, ground soybeans + water, often with sweetener, stabilisers, and fortification. Protein ~3.0 g/100ml; DIAAS ~1.08 — the only plant milk with protein quality matching or exceeding cow's milk per litre. Carries naturally occurring isoflavones (genistein, daidzein). BSACI-preferred for paediatric CMPA over 6 months, under dietetic supervision (see High-risk groups for the under-6m safety note).

(c) Almond. Made from ground almonds (typically 2–7% almonds by weight) + water + stabilisers. Calcium-fortified in most UK ranges. Protein ~0.5 g/100ml; DIAAS ~0.39 — lowest among major plant milks on both content and quality. The water-footprint outlier: ~371 L per L of almond milk globally; California-sourced ~500–600 L/L.

(d) Coconut. Made from coconut cream + water (or coconut milk + water). Highest saturated fat content among plant milks (~0.9–1.2 g/100ml). Protein ~0.5–1.0 g/100ml; DIAAS ~0.72. Calcium-fortified in most UK ranges. Common cultural-cuisine reference for South-East Asian, Caribbean, and South Indian traditions. Note: "coconut milk" (the cooking ingredient) sits on the closed Annex I exception list to the EU milk-name reservation, recognising long-standing traditional terminology; this is the only major plant beverage that may legally use "milk" on regulated UK/EU labels.

(e) Rice. Made from rice + water + enzymes (to convert starch to sugars) + stabilisers. Protein ~0.1 g/100ml; DIAAS ~0.43. Naturally contains free sugars from enzymatic conversion (the levy implication parallels the fruit juice intrinsic-but-now-free sugars case). The FSA's exact current position: "Rice drinks should not be used as a substitute for breast milk, infant formula, or cow's milk in children under 5 years old" (FSA, latest version reviewed March 2025), based on naturally occurring inorganic arsenic in rice.

(f) Hemp. Made from hemp seeds + water + stabilisers. Higher omega-3 (ALA) content than most plant milks. Lower UK market share; specialty product. Protein typically ~0.5–1.5 g/100ml; DIAAS not well-characterised in the published literature.

(g) Pea. Made from pea protein isolate + water + oil + stabilisers + fortification. Protein ~3.5 g/100ml; DIAAS 0.8–0.9. Higher protein content per 100ml than other plant milks except soy. BSACI's preferred-options list includes pea alongside soy and oat for paediatric CMPA in children over 12 months.

The single most consequential nutritional asymmetry across the seven: protein quality differs by a factor of ~3× from soy (DIAAS 1.08) to almond (DIAAS 0.39), and protein content differs by a factor of ~30× from pea (3.5 g/100ml) to rice (0.1 g/100ml). A consumer substituting cow's milk with soy loses ~25% of protein-quality-weighted contribution per litre; the same substitution with almond loses ~73% on quality and ~85% on absolute protein content. The shelf does not communicate this.

Citation transparency on DIAAS. The DIAAS values cited throughout this brief are derived from systematic-review compilations of protein quality studies on the base ingredients (soy protein isolate, oat protein, almond protein, etc.; FAO 2013 expert consultation; Reynolds and colleagues 2021; and similar reviews) and adjusted for the protein concentration of the finished beverage. Direct DIAAS measurement of commercial plant milks as sold is not available in the published literature as of May 2026. The values therefore reflect ingredient-level protein quality applied to beverage-level dilutions, not direct beverage measurements. The relative ranking (soy and pea top; almond and rice bottom) is robust across methods and reviews.

"Plant milk is just as good as cow's milk" conflates legal identity (different products) with nutritional substitution (depends entirely on fortification). "Almond milk is environmentally friendly" conflates greenhouse-gas footprint (lower than dairy) with water footprint (much higher than oat or soy, particularly for California-sourced almonds). "Soy milk causes hormone problems" conflates phytoestrogen chemistry with the observational evidence base, which points the other way in human populations.

The foundational framework

Three foundations that anchor every later claim.

Foundation 1: the EU/UK milk name reservation.

Regulation (EU) 1308/2013, Article 78(2) and Annex VII Part III, defines "milk" as "exclusively the normal mammary secretion obtained from one or more milkings, without either addition thereto or extraction therefrom." Annex VII Part III also reserves "milk products" — butter, cream, cheese, yoghurt, whey, kefir — for derivatives of that mammary secretion. The reservation applies to designation, marketing, and advertising. The CJEU in Tofu Town (C-422/16, 14 June 2017) ruled that the reservation precludes these terms for purely plant-based products even when qualified by clarifying language ("tofu butter," "veggie cheese," "soy milk"). The only exceptions are products listed on the closed Annex I to Commission Decision 2010/791/EU — a short list including "peanut butter" and "coconut milk" (long-standing traditional terms). UK plant-milk products are labelled "oat drink," "almond drink," "soya drink," "rice drink," etc. The word "milk" persists in informal trade communication and lay use; the legal name on the regulated front-of-pack is "drink."

Foundation 2: the fortification gap, with iodine as the headline concern.

Cow's milk delivers approximately 120 mg calcium / 100 ml, ~0.4–0.6 µg iodine / 100 ml (varying with cow's feed and season — winter UK milk typically higher than summer because of iodine-containing feed supplements), ~0.5 µg vitamin B12 / 100 ml, and ~3.2 g protein / 100 ml. Unfortified plant milks deliver substantially less of each of these except in the case of soy's protein content.

UK fortification rates (Clegg et al. 2023): 92% of non-organic UK plant milks are fortified with at least one micronutrient. Calcium: 87%. B12: 79%. Riboflavin (B2): 56%. Iodine: 34%. The iodine fortification rate is the load-bearing public-health gap. Cow's milk is the largest single dietary source of iodine in the UK; plant-milk substitution without iodine fortification measurably reduces iodine intake. Bath 2013 (offspring neurodevelopment), Bath 2017 (median iodine in UK milk-alternative drinks ~2% of cow's milk in non-fortified products), and Dineva 2022 (UK market survey: exclusive plant-milk consumers had significantly lower urinary iodine concentrations and were more likely to meet WHO criteria for iodine deficiency) form the load-bearing UK evidence chain.

Organic plant milks are typically not fortified. Organic certification standards restrict the addition of synthetic vitamins and minerals; many organic plant milks therefore deliver only the natural micronutrient content of the base ingredient. An organic almond drink may contain ~5–15 mg calcium / 100 ml versus ~120 mg / 100 ml for a non-organic fortified equivalent. For B12 specifically, the proportion of organic plant drinks without fortification approaches 100% in many product lines — a much larger gap than the 21% headline figure for the non-organic segment. EFSA is currently re-evaluating the maximum permitted voluntary fortification levels under retained EU 1925/2006, including for iodine; the current ceilings may change in 2025–2026 with implications for whether plant drinks can be fortified to match cow's milk's iodine concentration (cow's milk iodine is naturally occurring and not subject to the synthetic-fortification upper limits).

Cross-reference to the SACN free-sugars framework. Under SACN 2015 (see Fruit Juice for the full development), sugars released from the cellular structure of plant bases during processing — enzymatic conversion in rice drinks; milling and pasteurisation in oat drinks; pressing in almond and soy drinks — are classified as free sugars, parallel to the fruit juice case. This is the legal basis underpinning the proposed SDIL intrinsic-sugars carve-out. A "no added sugar" plant drink may still carry a measurable free-sugars load, again parallel to the 100% juice case.

Foundation 3: protein quality, content, and DIAAS.

Protein quality is a function of two variables: the digestibility of the protein and the amino acid pattern relative to human nutritional requirements. DIAAS (Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score) is the current FAO-recommended scoring method (since 2013); PDCAAS is the older method still used in many regulatory contexts.

1. The iodine gap

The load-bearing population-level public-health concern.

Bath et al. (Lancet, 2013), in the Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children (n = 1,040 pregnant women), found mild-to-moderate iodine deficiency during pregnancy was observationally associated with lower verbal IQ and reading scores in offspring at ages 8–9 years. The UK had been characterised as iodine-replete on the basis of older NDNS data, but the Lancet paper and subsequent analyses found population iodine intake had been declining, particularly in women of reproductive age. Cow's milk being the largest single dietary source meant plant-milk substitution was a documented contributor.

Bath et al. (2017) examined iodine concentrations in UK milk-alternative drinks specifically, finding median iodine concentrations approximately 2% of those in cow's milk in non-fortified plant drinks. Dineva et al. (2022, British Journal of Nutrition) extended this with a UK market survey: exclusive plant-milk consumers had significantly lower urinary iodine concentrations and were more likely to meet WHO criteria for iodine deficiency. The 34% fortification rate (Clegg et al. 2023) is the structural cause: most UK plant-milk products do not deliver the iodine load that cow's milk does, and the consumer-facing labelling does not flag this consistently.

Pending: EFSA fortification upper-limits re-evaluation. EFSA is currently re-evaluating maximum permitted voluntary fortification levels under retained EU 1925/2006, including for iodine. The current ceilings may change in 2025–2026 with implications for whether plant drinks can be fortified to match cow's milk's iodine concentration.

Decoder routing. Read the fortification panel; check for iodine specifically; if iodine is not listed, treat the product as not a full nutritional substitute for cow's milk in iodine terms. For pregnant women and women trying to conceive, route via NICE-aligned dietary advice and a registered dietitian.

2. Protein quality and the BSACI paediatric routing

The clinical reason soy and pea are BSACI-preferred over almond and rice.

For children with cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA), the British Society for Allergy and Clinical Immunology (BSACI) guidance routes management through specific milk-substitute hierarchies depending on age, severity, and breastfeeding status. The DIAAS protein-quality scores are the load-bearing variable.

BSACI's preferred plant-based options for paediatric CMPA where a plant drink is appropriate (children over 12 months): soy (DIAAS 1.08), pea (DIAAS 0.8–0.9), and oat (DIAAS 0.59) — provided the product is fortified and used as part of a complete dietary plan. Least preferred: almond (DIAAS 0.39) and rice (DIAAS 0.43; with the additional FSA arsenic position routing rice drinks out of the under-5 substitute role entirely).

Critical safety note on soy in CMPA. Soy is itself a major allergen. Approximately 10–14% of infants with CMPA also react to soy. For infants under 6 months with confirmed CMPA, the routing is not via plant drinks at all — it is via extensively hydrolysed formula or amino-acid-based formula under paediatric dietetic supervision. Soy-based infant formula (where used) is introduced under specialist guidance in some clinical contexts, but soy drinks (plant beverages, not infant formula) are not appropriate as primary nutrition for any infant under 12 months. The BSACI 2024 guidance and ESPGHAN parallel guidance both anchor this safety position.

Decoder routing. For paediatric populations, the plant-milk choice is a clinical-routing question, not a shelf-side preference question. Specific paediatric management routes through BSACI-aligned advice and a registered paediatric dietitian.

3. Calcium absorption and bone health

A well-shaken, well-fortified plant drink is clinically acceptable as a calcium source for the general adult population.

The published RCT evidence on calcium absorption from fortified plant milks vs cow's milk (Heaney 2000; Heaney, Rafferty and colleagues 2005; calcium absorption studies through the 2000s–2010s) finds that calcium fortification at the 120 mg/100ml level in plant milks delivers calcium absorption bioavailability comparable to cow's milk when the fortificant is calcium carbonate or calcium phosphate and the product is well-formulated. Absorption is reduced if calcium sediments in the carton (the consumer-facing instruction "shake well before use" is not cosmetic; sedimented calcium is not absorbed). Some studies have found marginally lower bioavailability from plant milks fortified with calcium carbonate compared with native milk calcium, but the differences are small (5–15%) and not consistently observed across studies.

The clinical implication: a well-fortified, well-shaken plant milk is a clinically acceptable calcium source for the general adult population. Bone health depends on the full dietary protein and vitamin D context, not on calcium alone; for at-risk populations (post-menopausal women, elderly, those with low bone mineral density), the routing remains through NICE-aligned bone-health advice and a registered dietitian.

4. Soy and breast cancer

The disavowed historical concern.

The hypothesis that soy isoflavones (the polyphenolic compounds genistein and daidzein, which bind weakly to estrogen receptors with greater affinity for ERβ than ERα) might exert clinically meaningful estrogenic effects with implications for breast cancer risk was an active research question through the 1990s and 2000s. The current peer-reviewed position has converged in the other direction.

Wei et al. (European Journal of Epidemiology, 2020), in a prospective cohort study of 300,000 Chinese women and an accompanying dose-response meta-analysis, found an inverse association between soy intake and breast cancer risk. Messina (Nutrients, 2016) systematic review found similar inverse associations and noted that intervention trials in breast cancer survivors do not show adverse effects. The WCRF/AICR Continuous Update Project (2018, updated 2023) similarly does not contraindicate soy for breast cancer prevention or survivorship. The consolidated position: soy isoflavone intake at levels consistent with traditional Asian dietary patterns is observationally associated with reduced breast cancer risk and is not contraindicated in breast cancer survivors.

Mechanism. At human dietary intake levels, the weak estrogen-receptor binding of isoflavones is below the threshold for estrogenic effect on mammary tissue, and the polyphenolic activity may exert offsetting anti-proliferative effects. The historical concern was based largely on in vitro and rodent-model studies at supraphysiological doses; the human cohort and intervention evidence has not replicated those concerns.

Decoder routing. Soy milk is a legitimate and safe plant-milk choice for the general adult population, including for women with personal or family history of breast cancer. For specific clinical contexts (active breast cancer treatment, hormone-receptor-positive disease, tamoxifen use), the routing is via the oncology team and a registered dietitian. The brief disavows the lay-discourse "soy is dangerous" framing as not supported by the current peer-reviewed evidence base.

5. The environmental footprint

GHG, land, water — and the almond outlier.

Poore & Nemecek (Science, 2018), in the largest food-system life-cycle assessment in the published literature (≈38,700 farms, 119 countries, 40 agricultural goods, ~570 source LCAs), provided the modern reference dataset for per-litre and per-kg comparisons across food categories. The structural reads.

  • Greenhouse-gas emissions: dairy is ~5–10× higher per litre than any plant milk. Load-bearing claim for the carbon-side environmental case for plant-milk substitution.
  • Land use: dairy is ~10–30× higher per litre than plant milks.
  • Water use: dairy is generally higher than plant milks, but almond is the exception — and California-sourced almond is the outlier within the outlier. California's almond industry consumes water equivalent to all urban household consumption in the state; the California Sustainable Groundwater Management Act 2014 implementation is the principal regulatory response. The "almond milk is environmentally friendly" claim is true for greenhouse-gas and land but not consistently true for water, particularly where water-scarcity weighting is applied or where the sourcing is California (which it predominantly is for UK retail almond drinks).
  • Rice has a higher water footprint than oat or soy — second only to almond among major plant milks — because of paddy-rice cultivation methods.

Decoder routing. Oat and soy are the lowest-environmental-footprint major plant milks across all three categories. Almond is low on GHG and land but high on water (especially California-sourced). Rice is moderate on GHG and land but high on water. Dairy is higher than all on GHG and land, and higher than most on water. The within-category variation is comparable to the between-category variation: a consumer making an environmental choice within plant milks (oat or soy over almond) may achieve a larger marginal environmental improvement than the dairy-to-plant switch alone.

6. The infant and toddler plant-milk question

NHS Start4Life and BSACI hierarchies by age.

  • Under 6 months: breast milk or infant formula. No plant drinks of any kind.
  • 6–12 months: breast milk or infant formula remains primary. Plant drinks may be used in cooking from 6 months if fortified (calcium ≥ 120 mg/100ml, ideally iodine) — but not as a main drink. Cow's milk also not appropriate as the main drink before 12 months for the same calorie- and protein-density reasons.
  • 12 months onwards: plant drinks may be used as the main drink if fortified and used as part of a balanced diet. NHS preferred options are unsweetened fortified soy, pea, or oat drinks. Almond and cashew are listed as least preferred due to lower protein.
  • Rice drinks: the FSA's exact position (current version, latest review March 2025): "Rice drinks should not be used as a substitute for breast milk, infant formula, or cow's milk in children under 5 years old." The basis is naturally occurring inorganic arsenic in rice. Rice drinks have no role as a substitute beverage in the under-5 diet.

Decoder routing for parents and carers. The plant-milk choice for under-12-month-olds is not a substitute-for-formula decision (formula or breast milk only); the plant-milk choice for 12-month-onwards children is a fortification-and-protein-content decision, with soy, pea, and oat preferred and almond/cashew deprioritised, and rice ruled out for under-5s entirely. The routing for any specific clinical context routes through a registered paediatric dietitian and NHS health-visiting team guidance, not via shelf-side preference.

UK regulatory frame in detail

The reservation, the levy, and the green-claims architecture.

EU Regulation 1308/2013 (retained UK law) is the reserved-name and compositional anchor for the milk name reservation. Article 78(2) and Annex VII Part III define "milk" as exclusively the normal mammary secretion of one or more milkings, without addition or extraction. The CJEU Tofu Town judgment (C-422/16, 14 June 2017) closed the question of whether qualified plant terms ("soy milk," "vegan cheese") could survive the reservation — the answer is no, except for products on the closed Annex I list. UK plant-milk products carry "drink" rather than "milk" on the regulated descriptor; "milk" remains permitted in brand voice and marketing copy where it does not function as the product designation. The legal-identity-rule layer is binding; trading standards enforces breaches.

The SDIL plant-drink reform is the most consequential regulatory development pending — but it is announced intention, not finalised law. The Soft Drinks Industry Levy (Finance Act 2017, in force April 2018) currently exempts milk-based drinks with ≥ 75 ml milk per 100 ml and plant-milk alternatives with ≥ 120 mg calcium / 100 ml — both exemptions resting on the public-health rationale of supporting calcium-and-protein-bearing beverages. The November 2025 HM Treasury / HMRC consultation outcome announced the intention to remove both exemptions with implementation 1 January 2028, with an intrinsic-sugars carve-out for sugars naturally present in or released during the manufacturing of the "core" plant ingredient (e.g. oat sugars in oat drink released by enzymatic processing). The structural shape of the carve-out parallels the existing 100% fruit juice intrinsic-sugars treatment.

Implementation status as of May 2026: the implementing regulations have not been published. The definition of "core" plant ingredient, the treatment of enzymatic conversion (rice drinks specifically), the threshold for "added" versus "intrinsic" sugars, and the overall scope of the carve-out remain under active drafting. Brief language throughout has been calibrated to reflect this status. Re-check in late 2027 / early 2028.

The SACN free-sugars classification applies to plant milks at the same level it applies to fruit juice. Plant-milk sugars released during enzymatic processing (rice drinks particularly) or naturally present in the base (oat sugars, coconut sugars, soy sugars) are free sugars under SACN 2015. The structural implication: a "no added sugar" plant drink may still carry a measurable free-sugars load, parallel to the 100% juice case.

The OHID Sugar Reduction Programme added milk-based drinks to its voluntary scope in May 2018, setting a 20% sugar reduction target by 2021. Plant drinks fall within scope of the milk-based-drinks reduction programme where they carry added sugars; unsweetened plant drinks are not within scope.

The Green Claims Code (CMA, 2021) applies to environmental marketing claims, including the "plant milks are environmentally friendly" framing common in plant-drink marketing. The Code requires claims to be truthful, clear, substantiated, and to consider the full life-cycle. The almond-milk water-footprint asymmetry documented by Poore & Nemecek (particularly for California-sourced almonds) means that a generic "almond milk is good for the environment" claim risks being misleading under the Code; specific claims tied to specific impact categories (e.g. "lower greenhouse gas emissions than dairy") are more defensible. The CMA's enforcement attention has been on textiles and fashion to date; plant-milk environmental claims have not yet been subject to high-profile enforcement, but the regulatory architecture is present.

International precedent

Four jurisdictions, four different anchor points.

United States (FDA). US labelling law is more permissive than the EU on plant-milk naming. "Almond milk," "soy milk," and similar terms are commonly used on US product labels. The FDA in 2018–2023 conducted a regulatory review on plant-milk labelling; the 2023 draft guidance proposed permitting plant-milk names with clarifying language about nutritional differences from cow's milk, rather than prohibiting the names outright. The US-EU divergence on milk-name reservation is the most consequential international labelling difference for plant milks.

Codex Alimentarius. Codex Standard CXS 206-1999 ("General Standard for the Use of Dairy Terms") parallels the EU position, reserving "milk" and dairy designations for products derived from mammary secretion. National implementations vary in strictness.

Chile (Ley 20.606, in force 2016) and Mexico (NOM-051, amended 2020) apply their black-octagon FOP warning systems to plant drinks on the same nutritional-threshold basis as other beverages. Sweetened plant drinks exceeding the sugar thresholds carry the "Alto en azúcares" or "Exceso de azúcares" octagons; unsweetened plant drinks generally do not.

Brazil (RDC 429/2020 and IN 75/2020, in force October 2022). Front-of-pack magnifying-glass labels for added sugars. Unsweetened plant drinks do not trigger the added-sugars label; sweetened plant drinks do.

EU plant-based food labelling proposals. The 2017 Tofu Town judgment closed one question (qualified plant terms cannot bypass the reservation); subsequent EU consultations and member-state initiatives have considered extending the reservation to meat designations ("veggie burger," "soy sausage"). As of May 2026, no further extension has been adopted at EU level; member-state divergences exist.

High-risk groups

Nine populations for whom the population-average reading does not apply.

1. Infants under 12 months — including the soy safety note.
Plant drinks (any base) are not appropriate as a main drink for infants under 12 months. Breast milk or infant formula remains primary. Plant drinks may be used in cooking from 6 months if fortified. For infants under 6 months with confirmed CMPA: soy should only be introduced under paediatric dietetic supervision due to ~10–14% cross-reactivity between soy and cow's milk protein; management routes through extensively hydrolysed or amino-acid-based formula under specialist guidance.
2. Children with cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA), ages 1–5.
BSACI's preferred plant-drink options where a plant drink is appropriate are soy, pea, and oat (fortified). Almond and cashew are listed as least preferred (lowest protein quality). Rice drinks (FSA position): "should not be used as a substitute for breast milk, infant formula, or cow's milk in children under 5 years old" — based on naturally occurring inorganic arsenic. Routing through a registered paediatric dietitian is the safe default.
3. School-age children and adolescents in the general population.
Plant drinks may be consumed as part of a balanced diet provided they are fortified. The NHS Eatwell Guide and School Food Standards do not prescribe specific products; the focus is on overall dietary pattern, calcium and iodine adequacy, and protein contribution. The fortification check (Ca ≥ 120 mg/100ml + iodine + B12) is the load-bearing decoder.
4. Pregnant women and women of reproductive age — the iodine concern (load-bearing).
Bath et al. (2013) and the Avon Longitudinal Study finding on mild-to-moderate iodine deficiency in pregnancy and offspring neurodevelopment is the load-bearing piece of evidence. Check iodine fortification specifically on any plant drink used as a cow's milk substitute during pregnancy and pre-conception, and route via NICE-aligned nutrition advice and a registered dietitian.
5. Vegans and others with no animal-source foods.
B12 fortification is the load-bearing micronutrient for this group. 21% of UK non-organic plant drinks are not B12-fortified; for organic plant drinks the proportion approaches 100% in many product lines. Decoder routing: choose a B12-fortified product or supplement separately.
6. The elderly and those with reduced appetite or marginal protein intake — sarcopenia framing.
Protein-quality differences across plant milks are most consequential for this population. Sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) is the clinical concern: maintenance of muscle mass in older adults depends on adequate dietary protein intake (typically 1.0–1.2 g protein per kg body weight per day for those over 65). Soy and pea drinks are protein-quality matched to or near cow's milk; almond and rice drinks deliver substantially less. Decoder routing for this population: the protein-content panel (aim for ≥ 3 g protein / 100 ml) and a registered dietitian where total dietary protein is a clinical concern.
7. People with multiple food allergies or intolerances.
Soy is one of the major allergens; almond and cashew are tree nuts (allergen). Coconut is treated as a tree nut for allergen-labelling purposes in some regulatory contexts (US FDA) but not others (UK/EU FIR allergen list). Pea protein may cross-react with peanut for some sensitised individuals. The routing is via a clinical allergist and dietetic input rather than via shelf-side preference.
8. People taking levothyroxine for hypothyroidism.
Soy may modestly reduce levothyroxine absorption when consumed close to the medication dose. The European Thyroid Association 2016 statement and Messina 2016 systematic review both find that separating soy intake from levothyroxine medication by at least 4 hours is sufficient (consistent with general guidance on fibre and supplements). No contraindication to moderate soy intake in treated hypothyroidism exists in the published evidence base. In iodine-replete individuals, dietary soy isoflavones do not cause clinically significant hypothyroidism.
9. Diaspora-community dietary contexts.
Plant milks have substantial cultural-cuisine variation across UK diaspora communities. Soy milk in Asian dietary patterns has a long traditional history. Coconut milk is a Caribbean, South Indian, and South-East Asian staple (and is one of the Annex I "permitted" traditional terms for which the EU reservation does not apply). Almond milk has Middle Eastern and Mediterranean culinary precedent. The cultural-cuisine framing recognises that plant milks are not a novel category in many diaspora communities; the regulatory and fortification framing developed in this brief is the UK-context-specific overlay, not a replacement for the cultural literacy already present.
Conflicts and uncertainties

Seven live contradictions in the evidence base.

Naming them strengthens the evidence base; hiding them weakens it.

1. The "almond milk is environmentally friendly" framing.

True on greenhouse-gas and land-use criteria; not consistently true on water-use criteria, particularly for California-sourced almonds where the water footprint is ~500–600 L per litre of beverage. A single-metric environmental claim about plant milks is misleading; the full Poore & Nemecek 2018 framework requires reporting across at least GHG, land, and water before a "good for the environment" claim is defensible. The Green Claims Code (CMA 2021) is the regulatory mechanism for enforcement.

2. The DIAAS-vs-PDCAAS protein-quality framing.

DIAAS is the FAO-recommended current method; PDCAAS is the older method still in regulatory use in many contexts. The DIAAS values cited in this brief are derived from systematic-review compilations of ingredient-protein-quality studies, adjusted for the protein concentration of the finished beverage. Direct DIAAS measurement of commercial plant milks as sold is not available in the published literature as of May 2026. The relative-ranking message (soy and pea top; almond and rice bottom) is consistent across both methods.

3. The soy-and-breast-cancer evidence.

The mainstream peer-reviewed position has converged on safety / inverse association. A minority of researchers continue to flag concerns at high-dose isoflavone supplementation (not dietary intake levels). The brief reflects the consensus position; specific clinical contexts (active breast cancer treatment, ER+ disease, tamoxifen interaction) route via oncology and dietetic input rather than via shelf-side preference.

4. The soy-and-thyroid concern.

Lay discourse periodically raises soy intake as a cause of hypothyroidism. The published position (Messina 2016; European Thyroid Association 2016): in iodine-replete individuals, dietary soy isoflavones do not cause clinically significant hypothyroidism. For those taking levothyroxine, separating intake by 4 hours is sufficient to avoid the modest absorption interaction. No contraindication to moderate soy intake in treated hypothyroidism exists in the current peer-reviewed evidence.

5. The iodine question and population-level public-health response.

The Bath 2013 Lancet paper, subsequent UK cohort analyses (Bath 2017; Dineva 2022), and the documented decline in UK iodine status are well-replicated. The public-health-policy response — whether to mandate iodine fortification of plant drinks at the population level, parallel to the mandatory bread/flour fortification regime — is a live debate. Action on Sugar, the Iodine Global Network, and the BDA have separately argued for mandatory iodine fortification of plant drinks; the UK government position as of May 2026 is voluntary industry compliance, not statutory mandate. EFSA's 2025–2026 re-evaluation of fortification upper limits may affect the technical scope for iodine fortification. The brief flags this as an open public-health-policy question without prescribing a specific policy outcome.

6. The 2028 SDIL plant-drink exemption removal.

The November 2025 HM Treasury / HMRC consultation outcome announced the intention to remove both the milk-based-drink exemption and the plant-drink-with-120 mg-calcium exemption, with proposed implementation 1 January 2028 and a proposed intrinsic-sugars carve-out for the core plant ingredient. Implementing regulations have not been published as of May 2026; the final scope of the carve-out is subject to change. The implementation details (which exact processing routes count as "core" for carve-out purposes; how rice-drink enzymatic sugars are treated; whether the carve-out covers added vegetable-base concentrates as sweeteners) are still being worked out. Re-verification due late 2027 / early 2028.

7. The DIAAS direct-measurement gap.

The published literature on plant-milk protein quality is grounded in ingredient-level studies (protein isolates, raw beans, raw oats) rather than direct measurement on commercial plant beverages as sold. Processing-induced changes (heat treatment, homogenisation, lecithin addition, stabiliser interactions) may shift the actual delivered protein quality of the finished beverage. The relative rankings are robust; the absolute DIAAS values cited should be treated as informed estimates rather than direct beverage measurements.

Decoder moves

Seventeen specific reads, split by context.

At the shelf.

  1. Read the regulated descriptor, not just the brand name. UK plant-milk products are labelled "oat drink," "almond drink," "soya drink" — not "oat milk." If the brand voice says "milk" but the regulated name says "drink," that's the legal product designation.
  2. Check the fortification panel before treating as a cow's milk substitute. Iodine first. Only 34% of non-organic UK plant drinks carry it; the proportion approaches 0% for organic. Then calcium ≥ 120 mg/100ml. Then B12. If any of the three are missing, the product is not a full nutritional substitute for cow's milk in those dimensions.
  3. Check the protein content for protein-dependent contexts. Cow's milk ~3.2 g/100ml. Soy ~3.0 g/100ml. Pea ~3.5 g/100ml. Oat ~1.0 g/100ml. Almond ~0.5 g/100ml. Rice ~0.1 g/100ml. For children over 12 months, the elderly (sarcopenia concern), and those with marginal total protein intake, the soy/pea route is preferred.
  4. For organic plant drinks: assume not fortified unless the panel proves otherwise. An organic almond drink at ~5–15 mg calcium/100ml is not a calcium-source substitute for cow's milk.
  5. Compare 100ml sugar values across sweetened vs unsweetened variants. Unsweetened oat: typically 4–6 g/100ml (intrinsic-but-now-free, parallel to fruit juice). Sweetened oat: 6–10 g/100ml. Sweetened chocolate / vanilla plant drinks: 8–12 g/100ml. The proposed SDIL 2028 reform (subject to implementing regulations) would levy the added portion, not the intrinsic.

At the kitchen.

  1. Shake before pouring. Calcium fortificant sediments; sedimented calcium is not absorbed. The "shake well before use" instruction on most fortified plant drinks is functional, not cosmetic.
  2. For children under 12 months: not as a main drink. Breast milk or infant formula remains primary. Fortified plant drink may be used in cooking from 6 months. For confirmed CMPA in infants, routing is via extensively hydrolysed or amino-acid formula under paediatric dietetic supervision; soy introduction in under-6m with CMPA requires specialist guidance due to 10–14% cross-reactivity.
  3. For children 1–5 years: fortified soy, pea, or oat (BSACI preferred) > almond or cashew. Rice drinks: FSA position is "should not be used as a substitute for breast milk, infant formula, or cow's milk in children under 5 years old" (arsenic basis).
  4. For pregnant women and women of reproductive age: prioritise iodine fortification. The Bath 2013 cohort finding on offspring neurodevelopment is the load-bearing evidence; iodine is the load-bearing micronutrient.
  5. For vegans and those without animal-source foods: B12 fortification check is critical. Either choose a B12-fortified product or supplement separately.
  6. For people taking levothyroxine for hypothyroidism: separate soy intake from medication by 4 hours. No contraindication to moderate soy intake; the absorption interaction is manageable by timing.

At the cultural-cuisine and equity dimensions.

  1. Traditional cultural-cuisine plant milks — coconut milk in Caribbean / South Indian / South-East Asian cooking, soy in East Asian cuisines, almond in Middle Eastern and Mediterranean traditions — carry both cultural value and bioactive value. The fortification gap argument applies to the modern UK regulated-trade product, not to traditional preparation. Cultural-cuisine literacy recognises both lineages.
  2. Lower-income household economics: dairy is typically cheaper per litre than fortified plant drinks. Semi-skimmed cow's milk £1.15–£1.45/L; unsweetened fortified oat drink £1.75–£2.50/L at 2026 UK supermarket prices. The cost differential is meaningful for budget-constrained households.
  3. Environmental claims discipline. "Plant milk is environmentally friendly" is too generic to be useful and may be misleading under the Green Claims Code (CMA 2021). Defensible specific claims: oat and soy drinks have lower greenhouse-gas emissions and lower land use than cow's milk; almond drink is similar on GHG and land but has higher water use, particularly from California sources.
  4. For school food procurement and commissioner contexts: the fortification specification matters. Procuring "plant drinks" without specifying fortification (calcium ≥ 120 mg/100ml, iodine, B12) risks lower nutritional substitution adequacy.

At the comparator-foods dimension.

  1. Calcium-set tofu as a high-bioavailability plant calcium source. Calcium-set tofu (made with calcium sulfate, E516) contains approximately 350 mg calcium per 100 g and is an excellent calcium source. Non-calcium-set tofu (made with magnesium chloride / nigari) contains ~60–80 mg calcium per 100 g. Decoder move: check the ingredient list for "calcium sulfate" (E516); if present, the tofu is a high-bioavailability calcium source comparable per gram to fortified plant milk and dairy. Fortified plant drink + calcium-set tofu + leafy greens is a defensible plant-based calcium-and-protein matrix.
  2. For clinicians and commissioners: routing to a registered dietitian for specific dietary advice on plant-milk substitution in pregnancy, paediatric care, elderly nutrition, sarcopenia management, and clinical conditions is the safe default. This brief decodes the evidence base and the regulatory architecture; specific clinical-dietary management routes through NICE-aligned advice, BSACI-aligned paediatric guidance, and registered-dietitian input.

Defamation-safety statement

What this brief does not claim.

This brief is a structural decoder of the regulatory architecture and peer-reviewed evidence base on plant-based milk alternatives in the UK context. It does not allege unlawful conduct by any named party. It does not assert that any specific manufacturer is in breach of fortification, labelling, or environmental-claim regulations beyond what is documented in the named peer-reviewed and institutional sources cited above. It does not state the proposed 2028 SDIL reform as settled law; the brief is calibrated throughout to distinguish announced intention from finalised regulation.

Named-party reference policy. Every reference to a specific commercial party (Oatly, Alpro, Innocent, Plenish, Mighty, Sproud, and others) is sourced to one of the following public-record categories: party's own published programmes; peer-reviewed evaluation literature naming the party; institutional publications naming the party; statutory instruments and primary legislation; authoritative trade-press coverage of party-disclosed activity; declared funding statements in peer-reviewed publications.

Cultural-accuracy commitment. Diaspora-community references in the equity and decoder sections are framed at the literacy-starting-point level. Specific cuisine traditions (East Asian soy, Caribbean and South Indian coconut, Middle Eastern and Mediterranean almond) contain substantial within-region variation; the brief names the patterns without claiming exhaustive coverage. Community-specific dietary guidance should be sought from registered dietitians and clinicians with cultural competence in the specific tradition.

Educational register positioning. SCANSMART is a food literacy and decision-support platform. It is not a medical device and does not provide medical advice. This brief sits at the educational layer of the FSMA Knowledge Library. Specific clinical-dietary management of any condition mentioned (CMPA, iodine deficiency in pregnancy, B12 deficiency, sarcopenia in the elderly, breast cancer survivorship, levothyroxine-treated hypothyroidism) should be guided by NICE-aligned clinical advice and registered-dietitian input, with paediatric routing through BSACI-aligned secondary care.
