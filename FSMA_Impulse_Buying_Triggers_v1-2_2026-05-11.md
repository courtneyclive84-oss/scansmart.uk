## Headline

The supermarket floor plan is engineered. Every shelf height, every end-aisle display, every checkout-line confectionery rack, every "was-now" sticker, every multibuy: designed outcomes. The design is paid for. The shopper standing in front of it is the target, not the negotiator. This brief decodes the manufacturer-engineered shopping environment — the categories of trigger you can spot, the peer-reviewed evidence base behind them, the UK regulatory frame that names some of them and exempts others, and what label literacy looks like when applied at the point of purchase.

The manufacturer-engineered structural play is this: the product on the eye-level shelf is on the eye-level shelf because someone paid for that real estate. The end-cap promotion you walk past on the way to the milk is end-cap because someone paid for that placement. The bright-coloured cereal at child-eye height is at child-eye height because someone paid for that placement. The retailer arranges the floor; the manufacturer pays for the slot. The shopper bears the consumption decision while bearing none of the design decision.

Naming the play is the first decoder move.

## Section 1: What "impulse" actually means in the trade literature

The retail industry distinguishes carefully between three terms that look identical from outside but mean very different things:

**Planned purchase** — the shopper enters the store with a specific intent to buy a specific item, executes that intent, and exits.

**Unplanned purchase** — the shopper buys something they had not explicitly planned to buy, but which they recognise as fitting a category they routinely consume (milk, bread, fruit).

**Impulse purchase** — the shopper buys something they had not planned to buy and which they had not previously identified as a category they wanted to consume on this trip. The decision happens at the shelf, triggered by an environmental cue.

The trade literature has tracked unplanned and impulse purchasing for decades. Successive industry surveys conducted by the Point-of-Purchase Advertising Institute (POPAI, now Shop!) have consistently estimated that a substantial majority of supermarket purchase decisions are made or finalised in-store rather than pre-planned at home (POPAI surveys, multiple iterations). Independent peer-reviewed research has produced more conservative but still substantial figures: Inman, Winer & Ferraro (2009), publishing in the *Journal of Marketing*, found that a large share of supermarket purchases were unplanned when defined strictly, with the proportion varying widely by category, shopper, and trip purpose.

Figures vary; the structural finding doesn't. **A significant share of what ends up in the trolley was not on the list.** That gap — between what the shopper planned to buy and what they actually bought — is the gap the retail design industry is built to widen.

## Section 2: The manufacturer/retailer interface — where the slot is bought

To understand the impulse-triggering environment, you have to look at the commercial interface between the manufacturer who makes the product and the retailer who sells it.

### 2.1 Slotting fees and trade allowances

In most modern grocery retail, manufacturers pay the retailer for shelf access. The most documented variant is the **slotting fee** (or *slotting allowance*) — a one-off or recurring payment from the manufacturer to the retailer in exchange for the retailer agreeing to stock a new product line. The Federal Trade Commission (US) investigated slotting allowances in the late 1990s and early 2000s and produced two extensive reports (FTC 2001, FTC 2003) documenting the practice as widespread across US grocery, drug, and mass-merchandise channels. The UK has historically been less explicit about slotting allowances than the US, but academic studies have documented analogous payments — listing fees, marketing development funds, promotional contributions, range-review charges — operating across major UK multiples (Bloom, Gundlach & Cannon 2000).

The structural consequence: shelf space is not allocated by retailer judgement about consumer benefit. It is allocated, in significant part, by the manufacturer's willingness and ability to pay. Large, well-capitalised manufacturers can buy more space; smaller manufacturers cannot. The shelf reflects the balance of capital, not the balance of nutrition.

### 2.2 Paid placement: end-caps, eye-level, checkout

Beyond simple shelf access, premium placement is bought separately:

**End-caps** (the displays at the ends of aisles) are among the highest-traffic real estate in any supermarket. Industry research has consistently found end-cap displays generate sales rates substantially higher than in-aisle equivalents (Curhan 1974, replicated extensively across subsequent decades).

**Eye-level shelf placement** is the most valuable inch in any aisle. Research summarising shelf-position effects has found products at adult eye-level (typically 150–160cm from the floor) sell at substantially higher rates than products at floor-level or top-shelf positions (Drèze, Hoch & Purk 1994; Sigurdsson, Larsen & Gunnarsson 2011).

**Checkout-aisle confectionery** sits at the highest-frustration, lowest-cognitive-resistance point of the shopping journey — the queue. Children are at child-eye-level relative to the confectionery rack while their parents are paying. This is now restricted in England for HFSS products under the 2021 Regulations (see Section 4).

In each case, the placement is **bought**. The manufacturer who pays the most ends up in the position that converts best. The retailer's interest is in revenue per square metre, which aligns with the manufacturer who can afford the slot.

### 2.3 The voice-rule restatement

Under the SCANSMART voice rule, the structural play sits with the manufacturer who designs the product to be commercially attractive and pays the retailer to position it for impulse capture. The retailer is the cooperating surface; the manufacturer engineers the gap. The shopper is positioned downstream of both.

This is consistent with the corrected canon banked 1 May 2026: when a product reaches the consumer in a way that overrides label-literate decision-making, the manufacturer is the creator of the gap. The retailer is the cooperating channel.

## Section 3: The five categories of trigger you can spot

Once the manufacturer/retailer interface is named, the consumer-side decoder move becomes practical. There are five broad categories of environmental trigger a shopper can learn to spot. None of them is hidden; all of them are designed.

### 3.1 Placement triggers

- **End-of-aisle displays.** The end-cap. Highest visibility, highest paid-placement value.
- **Eye-level shelf positioning.** Adults at 150–160cm; children at 90–110cm.
- **Checkout-aisle confectionery and impulse items.** The queue is the capture zone.
- **Store-entrance produce.** The first impression is fresh produce, which signals "wholesome environment" before the shopper reaches the centre aisles where the bulk of ultra-processed product sits (Hawkes 2008; Glanz et al. 2007).
- **Centre-aisle bottleneck.** The most-walked routes through the store are typically saturated with high-margin packaged goods.

### 3.2 Pricing triggers

- **BOGOF / multibuy structuring.** "Buy one get one free" and "3 for 2" promotions induce volume purchasing that often exceeds household consumption need (Mhurchu et al. 2010). The shopper buys more than they planned; the manufacturer moves more units. HFSS multibuy promotions are restricted in England under the 2021 Regulations from October 2025 (see Section 4).
- **"Was/now" pricing.** Anchoring against a reference price the shopper may never have paid.
- **Unit-price obscurement.** The per-100g or per-litre unit-price on the shelf-edge label, mandated under the UK Price Marking Order 2004, is the most important comparison number on the shelf. It is also typically in the smallest font.
- **Loyalty-card-only pricing.** Differential pricing where the lower price is available only to data-sharing loyalty card holders.

### 3.3 Sensory triggers

- **In-store bakery aroma.** Olfactory cues at retail entrances and bakery sections are a documented sensory trigger (Spence 2015; Gueguen & Petr 2006).
- **Music tempo.** Slower music has been associated with longer dwell time and higher per-trip spend in retail research (Milliman 1982; Caldwell & Hibbert 2002).
- **Lighting and colour.** Warm lighting on produce, cool lighting on dairy, dramatic spotlighting on bakery and butchery sections.

### 3.4 Packaging triggers

- **Front-of-pack health-halo claims.** "Wholegrain," "Source of fibre," "Natural," "No added sugar." Each is regulated; each is also frequently used in ways that imply more than they legally claim. Decoded in detail in companion decoder `library-nutrition-claims`.
- **Character licensing and cartoon mascots.** The structural play here is documented internationally (Smith, Kelly, Yeatman & Boyland 2019 — *Nutrients*; Cairns, Angus & Hastings 2009 — WHO commissioned review). Character branding has been shown to raise children's preference for and intake of branded products, particularly when the branding is on high-fat / high-sugar / high-salt products.
- **Colour psychology.** Bright colours, gloss finishes, large typography for claims, small typography for ingredient lists.

### 3.5 Promotional / digital triggers

- **Email and app push-prompts.** Personalised "you might like" reminders triggered by past purchase patterns.
- **Online basket nudges.** "Customers who bought this also bought…" Algorithmic placement that mimics paid end-cap logic in the physical store.
- **Dynamic personalised pricing.** Different prices for different customers based on inferred willingness-to-pay (the practice Maryland HB 895 prohibits as of May 2026).
- **Scarcity prompts.** "Only 3 left in stock." Genuine in some cases, manufactured in others.
- **Subscription default-on.** "Save 10% with subscribe and save."

## Section 4: UK regulatory frame

The UK has, since 2022, introduced the most substantive regulatory restrictions on the engineered shopping environment seen in any major Western economy. The frame is still incomplete, contested in places, and was partially delayed in implementation — but it exists, and it is current as of 11 May 2026.

### 4.1 The Food (Promotion and Placement) (England) Regulations 2021

Statutory Instrument 2021 No. 1368. Came into force **1 October 2022**. The regulations apply in England (Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland have devolved competence and have not adopted identical instruments at the time of writing).

**Placement restrictions (in force from October 2022).** Pre-packaged High Fat, Salt and Sugar (HFSS) products cannot, in qualifying medium and large retail businesses, be placed at:

- Store entrances
- Aisle ends (end-caps)
- Checkout zones
- Equivalent online positions on the homepage, on category landing pages, and at the digital checkout

The HFSS classification is determined by the **Nutrient Profile Model (NPM)** — originally the 2004/2005 FSA NPM, updated in 2023/2024 with a free-sugars adjustment that remains subject to a future implementation consultation (covered in SCANSMART External Scan watch list item 15).

**Volume-promotion restrictions (multibuy, BOGOF on HFSS).** Originally scheduled for October 2022 alongside placement restrictions but delayed multiple times under successive governments. As of the most recent confirmed implementation, the volume-promotion restrictions on HFSS products came into force **October 2025**.

### 4.2 ASA / CAP HFSS advertising rules

Following the implementation timeline updates and confirmed in force from **January 2026**, the Advertising Standards Authority and Committee of Advertising Practice rules restrict HFSS food and drink product advertising in TV (pre-watershed) and paid-for online advertising.

The first enforcement rulings under the new advertising regime appeared in April 2026 (SCANSMART External Scan 4 May 2026 — public ASA rulings of 15 April 2026 were the first under the new rules).

### 4.3 Devolution divergence

- **Wales.** The Welsh Government has consulted on equivalent placement restrictions and has a stated policy intent but had not, at the time of writing, brought forward an equivalent statutory instrument with identical scope. Watch the Welsh Food Advisory Committee meeting of 18 May 2026 (SCANSMART External Scan 9 May 2026 item 6).
- **Scotland.** The Scottish Government has separately consulted on restrictions on the promotion and marketing of food and drink high in fat, sugar or salt.
- **Northern Ireland.** Subject to ongoing review.

### 4.4 What the regulation names — and what it doesn't

The UK regulatory frame names a substantial subset of impulse triggers — paid placement of HFSS at entrances, end-caps, and checkouts; volume promotions on HFSS; HFSS advertising in specified channels. It does not currently name:

- Eye-level placement (still entirely market-determined)
- Character licensing and mascot use on the physical pack at point of purchase (the HFSS advertising rules cover some channels but not all surfaces)
- Loyalty-card-only pricing
- Dynamic personalised online pricing (the Maryland HB 895 territory)
- Sensory triggers (aroma, music, lighting)

The decoder-literacy implication: **regulation closes some of the engineered triggers, not all.** The shopper who relies on regulation alone to mediate the shopping environment is exposed on the unregulated triggers. The shopper who has learned to spot all five categories independently is mediated on every trigger.

## Section 5: International precedent and parallel moves

### 5.1 Maryland HB 895 (May 2026)

Maryland Governor Wes Moore signed the Protection from Predatory Pricing Act on **6 May 2026**, prohibiting large food retailers (15,000+ sq ft) and third-party delivery providers from using dynamic pricing or personal data to set higher prices, and restricting the use of "protected class data" (race, gender, ethnicity) to differentiate pricing. More than two dozen US states are reportedly considering similar legislation. (SCANSMART External Scan 9 May 2026, item 9.)

The relevance to UK readers: this is the regulatory frontier on the digital-shelf side of the engineered environment. It is the kind of intervention the UK has not yet made. As more UK grocery moves online — the NIQ-FMI 2026 report projects US online grocery to reach $452B by 2028 — the digital-shelf trigger surface becomes a larger share of the total shopping environment.

### 5.2 WHO recommendations on marketing food and non-alcoholic beverages to children

The WHO has, since 2010, issued and updated recommendations on the marketing of food and non-alcoholic beverages to children (WHO 2010; WHO 2023 update). The recommendations include restrictions on marketing in settings where children gather and on the use of techniques (premium offers, character licensing, celebrity endorsement) targeting children. National implementation varies widely.

### 5.3 Chile, Mexico, and the front-of-pack warning-label precedent

Chile introduced front-of-pack black octagon warning labels in 2016 under the Law of Food Labelling and Advertising (Law 20.606). Subsequent peer-reviewed evaluations (Taillie et al. 2020 — *PLOS Medicine*; Reyes et al. 2020 — *PLOS Medicine*) found measurable reductions in consumer purchasing of products carrying warning labels. Mexico introduced a similar regime in 2020. The UK does not currently use warning-label-style front-of-pack — the traffic-light system is informational rather than warning-coded — but the international evidence base for warning labels is now substantial.

## Section 6: The digital shelf — dark patterns in online grocery

The trigger architecture is migrating online. The categories of trigger map across with minimal modification:

| Physical-shelf trigger | Digital-shelf equivalent |
| --- | --- |
| End-cap placement | Homepage carousel / category page top slot |
| Eye-level shelf | "Recommended for you" position above the fold |
| Multibuy / BOGOF | "Add 2 more to save £3" basket prompts |
| Was/now sticker | Strikethrough RRP next to current price |
| Checkout confectionery | "Add to your basket?" upsell at digital checkout |
| Character licensing | Algorithmic recommendations to family accounts |
| Loyalty-card-only pricing | "Members-only" digital pricing |
| In-store aroma / sensory | High-resolution food photography, video |

The structural difference is that the digital shelf can do **per-shopper personalisation** in a way the physical shelf cannot. The end-cap is the same end-cap for every shopper. The "Recommended for you" slot is bespoke. This widens both the manufacturer/retailer revenue potential and the consumer-side asymmetry.

The UK has not yet regulated digital-shelf placement to the same standard as physical-shelf placement under the 2021 Regulations. Watch the FSA Future of Food Regulation programme detailed workplan, scheduled for the June 2026 FSA Board meeting (SCANSMART External Scan watch list item 13).

## Section 8: The decoder-literacy move — what the shopper can spot

The point of this brief is not to argue that the shopping environment is malevolent. It is engineered, which is a different thing. Engineering can be observed and accounted for. The CheckIT / SCANSMART decoder move is to give the shopper the same map the retailer and manufacturer already have.

At the shelf, here is what you can spot:

1. **Look up and down.** Eye-level is bought. Floor-shelf and top-shelf are not. Cheaper, less-promoted product is typically lower or higher than your line of sight.
2. **Check the per-100g unit price.** It is on the shelf-edge label by law (Price Marking Order 2004) in tiny print. Compare per-100g, not per-pack.
3. **Treat end-caps as advertisements.** They are. End-cap product is end-cap because it converts, not because it is better value.
4. **Read multibuy maths.** "3 for 2" is only a saving if you would have bought three. If you would have bought one, "3 for 2" is a 100% spend increase.
5. **Trust the ingredient list over the front-of-pack claim.** Every regulated nutrition claim ("low fat", "no added sugar", "source of fibre", "natural", "wholegrain") is decoded in `library-nutrition-claims`. The ingredient list is the harder document for the manufacturer to dress up.
6. **Notice what is at child-eye-level.** If you are shopping with a child, the confectionery and cereal at 90–110cm from the floor are at your child's eye-level. This is documented practice. Pre-emptive routing is the parent's countermove.
7. **Online: be sceptical of the basket nudge.** "Customers also bought" is the algorithmic end-cap.
8. **Online: log out and re-check the price.** Loyalty-card-pricing and personalised offers are surfaced to logged-in users. The logged-out price is the baseline.

These are not hacks. They are normal label and shelf literacy applied at the trigger point.

## Section 9: Voice rule application — naming the structural play

Per the SCANSMART voice canon corrected on 1 May 2026: **the manufacturer is the creator of the gap.**

In the impulse-trigger context, the gap is between the shopper's planned purchase set and the shopper's actual purchase set. The retailer is the cooperating surface — the floor plan, the shelf, the checkout, the website — but the floor plan is shaped by what manufacturers will pay to occupy it. Without the manufacturer's payment for the slot, the slot doesn't exist as a high-conversion impulse position. Without the manufacturer's pack design, the slot has no high-margin product to fill it.

The decoder move is not to blame the retailer for stocking what manufacturers pay them to stock. The decoder move is to **name the manufacturer as the upstream actor and the retailer as the cooperating surface**, and to give the shopper the literacy to navigate both.

This holds across surfaces: the same naming applies to the physical store, the website, the loyalty app, and the personalised email. It is the cross-surface voice rule encoded in §92 Dr RooT (root = superuser).
